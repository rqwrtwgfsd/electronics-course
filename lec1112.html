Lec 11 — Scenario 1: MCU Resets When Motor Turns On

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 1: MCU Resets When Motor Turns On

SCENARIO 1: "My microcontroller resets randomly when the motor turns on" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] MCU works perfectly until motor starts. At motor start: MCU randomly resets or behaves erratically. No obvious wiring problem. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> MCU resets = its VCC voltage briefly drops below threshold -> Motor draws huge inrush current at startup -> This current causes voltage sag on shared power rail Q2: Time or frequency problem? -> TIME problem. The sag lasts microseconds to milliseconds. Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem. VCC is sagging. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Need something that SUPPLIES extra current during the sag. -> Must be connected IN PARALLEL with MCU power pin (VCC-GND). Q5: DC or AC? -> Motor noise is AC transient on DC rail. Q6: How fast? -> Motor inrush = microseconds. Need fast response. [SOLUTION] TWO capacitors in PARALLEL with MCU VCC pin: Cap 1: 10uF electrolytic (bulk storage -- handles slow sag) Cap 2: 100nF ceramic (fast transient -- handles us spikes) BONUS: Ferrite bead in SERIES between motor supply and MCU supply. -> High impedance at HF, low DCR for DC. FINAL TOPOLOGY: Motor supply --[Ferrite Bead]---- MCU VCC | | 10uF 100nF | | GND GND [KEY INSIGHT] Capacitor in PARALLEL = voltage stabilizer / local reservoir. Inductor/ferrite in SERIES = noise blocker. Use BOTH together for robust power integrity.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The decoupling capacitors act as a small local water tank next to the MCU. When the motor pump suddenly demands high current and drops main pressure, the tank releases its stored charge instantly, maintaining local voltage. The ferrite bead is a shock-wave valve: it lets steady DC flow freely but blocks the high-frequency transient from propagating backward into the MCU supply node.

🔬 Physics

Motor inrush causes a step change in current demand. PCB trace inductance L produces V = L×dI/dt transient sag. The 100nF ceramic capacitor has ESL ~0.5nH, enabling sub-nanosecond response, while the 10uF electrolytic stores 100× more energy (E = 0.5CV²) for millisecond-scale sags. Together they cover 6 orders of magnitude in time constant.

📐 Math

Sag condition: \( \Delta V = \frac{I_{inrush} \cdot t_{sag}}{C_{total}} \)

Brownout prevention: \( \Delta V < V_{CC} - V_{brownout} \)

Capacitor energy: \( E = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 \)

Ferrite impedance (Murata BLM18AG601): \( |Z|(100\,\text{MHz}) \approx 600\,\Omega \)

🔧 Practical

Use 10uF X5R 0805 + 100nF X7R 0402, within 2mm of MCU VCC pin. Ferrite bead: Murata BLM18AG601SN1D (600ohm@100MHz, 2A rated). For 3.3V MCU (brownout 2.7V): C_min = I_inrush × t_sag / 0.6V. If I_inrush = 2A and t_sag = 1ms: C_min = 3.3mF (use 4×1000uF in parallel!)

📖 Story

In 2018, a Bosch automotive transmission ECU team traced random resets during cold starts to a 300A starter motor sag on the 12V bus. The fix was two 47uF X5R 1210 ceramics within 5mm of each ECU power connector, plus a 600ohm ferrite bead. The root cause was decoupling caps placed 40mm from the VCC pins, adding 40nH of trace inductance that completely negated their fast-response benefit at the moment of inrush.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope probe on MCU VCC pin, trigger on MCU reset pin. Capture the voltage waveform at motor start. Step 2: Measure minimum voltage — compare to brownout threshold from datasheet. Step 3: Add 10uF ceramic temporarily (clip leads), observe if reset stops. Step 4: Probe both sides of ferrite bead: HF noise should appear before bead, reduced after. Step 5: Check ESR of electrolytic with LCR meter — dry cap shows ESR >1ohm at 100kHz.

🚀 Optimize

For production: use a dedicated LDO regulator for the MCU supply. An LDO provides active regulation, not passive filtering. Consider replacing the electrolytic with a polymer aluminum capacitor (lower ESR, no dry-out failure mode, longer lifetime at high temperature). For harsh automotive: add TVS diode (SMBJ5.0A) in parallel with capacitors to clamp spikes above 5.5V.

Lec 11 — Scenario 2: Noisy ADC Readings from Sensor

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 2: Noisy ADC Readings from Sensor

SCENARIO 2: "My sensor reading fluctuates wildly -- noisy ADC values" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Temperature sensor connected to MCU ADC. Expected: stable reading ~512 counts. Actual: reading jumps 480-540 randomly every sample. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> ADC reading is noisy = noise on analog signal line. Q2: Time or frequency? -> FREQUENCY problem. High-frequency noise riding on slow signal. -> Temperature changes slowly (DC/very low freq). -> Noise is high frequency (switching, EMI). Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem. Signal voltage is contaminated. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Need to PASS the low-frequency signal. -> Need to BLOCK or DIVERT the high-frequency noise. -> Capacitor in PARALLEL (to GND) = low-pass filter. -> Resistor in SERIES = forms RC with parallel cap. Q5: DC or AC? -> Signal is DC/very low freq. Noise is AC (high freq). Q6: How fast? -> Signal changes at maybe 1Hz (temperature). -> Noise is at maybe 1MHz (switching supply). -> Set f_c between them: maybe 100Hz. [SOLUTION] RC LOW-PASS FILTER: Sensor --[R=1k]---- ADC Pin | C=1.5uF | GND f_c = 1/(2*pi*1k*1.5u) = 106Hz WHY R IN SERIES? -> Forms the "top half" of a frequency-dependent divider. -> Also protects ADC pin from ESD. WHY C IN PARALLEL (to GND)? -> At high frequency: C = short circuit -> noise goes to GND. -> At low frequency: C = open circuit -> signal passes to ADC. [KEY INSIGHT] Series R + Parallel C = Low-pass filter. This is the most common analog signal conditioning circuit in existence. Every ADC input in a real product has this.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The RC low-pass filter is a frequency-selective water valve. Low-frequency signals (your actual temperature data) are heavy and slow: they cannot pass through the side pipe (the capacitor to GND) because at low frequencies the capacitor looks like a closed valve. High-frequency noise is light and fast: it easily escapes down through the capacitor to GND before it can reach the ADC pin.

🔬 Physics

The capacitor's impedance is Z_C = 1/(jωC). At low frequencies (ω small), Z_C is very large — the capacitor is nearly open circuit, and the signal passes through R to the ADC with minimal attenuation. At high frequencies (ω large), Z_C approaches zero — the capacitor becomes a short circuit, diverting noise to GND. The voltage divider formed is: V_ADC = V_in × Z_C / (R + Z_C).

📐 Math

Transfer function: \( H(f) = \frac{1}{1 + j\frac{f}{f_c}} \)

Corner frequency: \( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi RC} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 1\text{k} \times 1.5\mu} = 106\,\text{Hz} \)

Attenuation at noise (1MHz): \( |H| = \frac{f_c}{f_{noise}} = \frac{106}{1\text{M}} = 0.0106\% \) remaining → 99.99% noise removed

Time constant: \( \tau = RC = 1\text{k} \times 1.5\mu\text{F} = 1.5\,\text{ms} \)

🔧 Practical

Component values for 106Hz corner: R = 1k (0402, 1%), C = 1.5uF X5R 0805 (or 2×680nF X7R 0402 in parallel). Place C physically at ADC pin, not at sensor. The R also provides ESD protection for the ADC pin. For MCU with internal 10k sample-and-hold: the external 1k series R plus the internal 10k forms a divider — account for 9% gain error, or use a buffer op-amp between filter and ADC.

📖 Story

In 2021, the Honeywell HVAC controller team was shipping thermostats with temperature readings that jumped by 3-5 degrees Fahrenheit every time the heating element switched on. The switching power supply for the heating relay ran at 200kHz, and its harmonics reached 1MHz on the supply rail and capacitively coupled into the NTC thermistor signal wire routed nearby. The fix was a 4.7k series resistor and 330nF capacitor (f_c = 102Hz) at the MCU ADC pin, reducing ADC noise from 30 counts to 2 counts.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Measure ADC reading with a known stable voltage at input (replace sensor with 1.65V reference). If reading is still noisy, the MCU ADC or its power supply is the problem. Step 2: Connect oscilloscope to sensor output node (before R). If noise is visible here, the sensor wiring is picking up EMI. Step 3: Move scope probe to ADC side of R: noise should be attenuated. If not, R value is too small or C is not properly grounded. Step 4: Check C is connected to clean analog GND, not digital GND.

🚀 Optimize

Use a differential ADC (two inputs: signal and a reference at same impedance) to reject common-mode noise. For precision measurements, use an instrumentation amplifier (INA) before the ADC to amplify the signal (increasing SNR before the filter) and reject common-mode interference. Oversampling and decimation in firmware (averaging N samples) provides an additional 3dB of SNR improvement per factor of 2 in sample rate.

Lec 11 — Scenario 3: Button Bounce (Multiple Triggers)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 3: Button Bounce (Multiple Triggers)

SCENARIO 3: "When I press the button, the action happens 5-10 times instead of once" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Button connected to MCU GPIO. Single press triggers 5-10 interrupts. Code is correct. Problem is physical. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> Button BOUNCING. Mechanical contacts bounce 5-10ms when pressed. -> Each bounce = separate rising/falling edge -> multiple triggers. Q2: Time or frequency? -> TIME problem. Need to IGNORE signal changes for ~10ms after first press. Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem. Signal bouncing between HIGH and LOW. Q4: Series or parallel? -> RC circuit: capacitor SMOOTHS the transitions. -> R in SERIES with signal path. -> C in PARALLEL to GND. -> Together: signal takes tau=RC time to transition. Q5: DC or AC? -> Bounce frequencies: ~1kHz-10kHz. -> Want to kill anything faster than ~50Hz. Q6: How fast should response be? -> tau > bounce duration (10ms) -> set tau ~10-20ms. [SOLUTION] Button --[R=10k]---- GPIO Pin | | GND C=1uF | GND tau = 10k x 1u = 10ms WHY THIS WORKS: -> Button presses: voltage rises slowly over 10ms (capacitor charges) -> MCU sees clean, slow rising edge -- not the fast bounces -> Bounces (sub-ms events) do not affect output (tau >> bounce duration) [KEY INSIGHT] RC debounce = using tau to create a "window of blindness" that is longer than the noise but shorter than real events. tau must satisfy: bounce_duration << tau << press_duration

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The RC debounce circuit creates a "reaction time" for the MCU. Imagine a ball bouncing on a table (the button contact). Each bounce lasts 1ms. But you only care whether the ball is on the table or not, and you only want to check after it has settled (10ms). The capacitor is your "memory": it slowly charges up as the button is held down, ignoring the brief disconnections of each bounce, and only crosses the logic threshold after the settling time has passed.

🔬 Physics

The capacitor charges through R with time constant tau = RC. During a bounce (button opens for 0.5ms), the capacitor begins to discharge, but only drops by V × (1 - e^(-t_bounce/tau)) = V × (1 - e^(-0.5ms/10ms)) ≈ 4.9% — far too small to cross the logic threshold. The MCU sees a smooth, monotonically rising voltage instead of rapid on-off pulses. The Schmitt trigger input of most MCU GPIO pins provides hysteresis (typically 0.2V × VCC) to further clean up slow edges.

📐 Math

Debounce time constant: \( \tau = RC = 10\text{k} \times 1\mu\text{F} = 10\,\text{ms} \)

Voltage change during bounce: \( \Delta V = V_{charge} \times (1 - e^{-t_{bounce}/\tau}) \approx 4.9\% \)

Rule: \( t_{bounce} \ll \tau \ll t_{press} \)

Typical values: \( t_{bounce} = 1{-}10\,\text{ms} \), \( t_{press} = 100\,\text{ms} \), therefore \( \tau = 10{-}20\,\text{ms} \)

🔧 Practical

Components: 10k 0402 1% + 1uF X5R 0805. For 3.3V MCU: GPIO input threshold ~1.65V. Time to reach threshold from 0V: t = tau × ln(VCC / (VCC - Vth)) = 10ms × ln(3.3/1.65) = 6.9ms — input recognized 6.9ms after press. Maximum debounce time: tau = 20ms still gives 100ms/(5×20ms) = 1 detection per 100ms = 10 button presses per second, sufficient for human input.

📖 Story

In 2020, the Siemens industrial HMI team received field reports of random double-actuation of emergency stop buttons in conveyor belt systems. The buttons were physically robust but had wear on the silver contacts, causing bounce durations up to 15ms. The original design used 1k + 100nF (tau = 0.1ms) — completely inadequate. The fix was 10k + 2.2uF (tau = 22ms), and the double-actuation problem was eliminated across the installed base of 1,200 machines.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope on GPIO pin with single-shot trigger on falling edge. Zoom in to see individual bounces. Step 2: Measure bounce duration — this sets minimum tau. Step 3: Add R and C, verify on scope that the transition is now a single smooth curve. Step 4: Verify logic level reaches VCC (not limited by R divider with internal pull-up, if any). Step 5: Test maximum press rate — ensure debounce does not miss rapid-fire inputs.

🚀 Optimize

For very fast-response applications (gaming peripherals, musical instruments), use firmware debounce instead of or in addition to hardware. Sample the GPIO every 1ms in a timer interrupt; require N consecutive identical readings before accepting a state change. This gives N ms of debounce with zero component cost. Combine hardware RC (tau = 3ms) with firmware debounce (3 samples) for industrial reliability.

Lec 11 — Scenario 4: Audio Amplifier Hum (Power Supply)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 4: Audio Amplifier Hum (Power Supply)

SCENARIO 4: "My audio amplifier has a loud hum even with no input signal" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Audio amplifier connected to speaker. When no music playing: audible 50Hz/60Hz hum from speaker. Power supply is from wall adapter. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> 50/60Hz hum = power line frequency contaminating audio. -> Ripple at 100/120Hz (full-wave rectified) on DC supply. Q2: Time or frequency? -> FREQUENCY problem. 100Hz noise on DC rail. Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem. Supply voltage has ripple. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Large capacitor in PARALLEL -> absorbs ripple. -> Inductor in SERIES -> blocks ripple. [SOLUTION] PART A -- Fix power supply ripple: Rectifier output --[L=100mH]---- Clean DC out | 1000uF | GND PART B -- Block DC from audio signal path: Amplifier --[C=10uF]---- Speaker (Coupling capacitor: blocks DC, passes 20Hz-20kHz audio) [KEY INSIGHT] Parallel C = filter capacitor (absorbs ripple from supply) Series C = coupling capacitor (blocks DC from signal path) Understanding which ROLE a component plays is critical.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The LC power filter is like a dam with a calm reservoir downstream. The rushing, turbulent river (rectified AC with 100Hz ripple) enters the dam. The series inductor is the dam wall that resists the rapid flow changes (it stores magnetic energy and releases it smoothly). The parallel capacitor is the reservoir that absorbs excess energy during current peaks and releases it during troughs. What exits downstream is a calm, smooth DC flow. The coupling capacitor is a completely different role: it is a one-way pass filter in the signal path, letting audio frequencies through while blocking the DC bias level.

🔬 Physics

The ripple at 100Hz (full-wave rectified 50Hz mains) has amplitude V_ripple = I_load / (2 × f × C) for a simple capacitor filter. Adding the series inductor L creates an LC filter with cutoff f_0 = 1/(2pi*sqrt(LC)). Below f_0, both L and C allow the signal to pass; above f_0, the inductive impedance Z_L = jωL increases while capacitive impedance Z_C = 1/(jωC) decreases — both fighting the ripple from opposite directions for a second-order -40dB/decade rolloff.

📐 Math

Ripple voltage (cap-only filter): \( V_{ripple} = \frac{I_{load}}{2fC} \)

LC filter corner: \( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{0.1 \times 1000\mu}} \approx 15.9\,\text{Hz} \)

Attenuation at 100Hz: \( A = \left(\frac{f}{f_0}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{100}{16}\right)^2 \approx 39\times = 32\,\text{dB} \)

Coupling cap high-pass: \( f_{c,HP} = \frac{1}{2\pi R_{load} C_{couple}} \)

🔧 Practical

Power filter: use 100mH toroidal inductor (Bourns SRR1260-101Y, 100mH, 500mA) + 1000uF 35V electrolytic (Nichicon UVR series). Coupling cap: for 8ohm speaker, C_couple must give f_c << 20Hz: C = 1/(2pi*8*20) = 1000uF minimum. In practice: 2200uF electrolytic (Nichicon UKW). Note coupling cap polarity: positive terminal toward amplifier output (higher DC potential).

📖 Story

In 2019, a small guitar amplifier manufacturer (Catalinbread, Portland OR) received customer complaints of 60Hz hum only on units shipped to the US market (60Hz mains). Investigation revealed the power transformer had insufficient filtering after rectification. The original 470uF filter capacitor was replaced with 2200uF + 10mH choke (f_0 = 34Hz), reducing ripple from 120mV peak-to-peak to under 2mV. The coupling capacitor from the preamp to the power amp was also increased from 1uF to 10uF to prevent low-bass phase shift.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Connect oscilloscope to amplifier output (speaker terminals). If you see 100Hz sinusoid without any audio input, it is supply ripple. Step 2: Probe the DC supply rail — measure peak-to-peak ripple. Greater than 50mV at 100Hz is excessive for audio. Step 3: With signal generator at 100Hz, verify the amplifier correctly amplifies it (to confirm the hum is supply, not a feedback issue). Step 4: Check coupling cap has correct polarity and not shorted (ESR meter). Step 5: Verify LC filter is wired correctly — L must be in series, not shunted.

🚀 Optimize

Replace electrolytic coupling caps with film capacitors (polypropylene or polyester) for lower distortion and longer lifetime. Film caps have no electrolytic degradation and much lower equivalent series resistance at audio frequencies. For the power filter: a two-stage LC (L1-C1-L2-C2) gives fourth-order filtering (–80dB/decade) — dramatically better ripple rejection at the cost of two additional components.

Lec 11 — Scenario 5: Long Cable Ringing (Signal Integrity)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 5: Long Cable Ringing (Signal Integrity)

SCENARIO 5: "My circuit works on the bench but oscillates/rings when I connect long cables" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Circuit output is clean on oscilloscope at PCB. When a 2-meter cable is connected to the output: -> Signal shows ringing (oscillation) at transitions. -> Sometimes output is completely wrong. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> Cable has INDUCTANCE (L) and CAPACITANCE (C) per unit length. -> Long cable = significant L and C -> forms transmission line. -> Unmatched impedance -> signal reflects back -> ringing. Q2: Time or frequency problem? -> FREQUENCY problem (ringing at specific frequency). -> Also TIME problem (ringing lasts several nanoseconds). Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem (voltage ringing on signal). Q4: Series or parallel? -> Series resistor at OUTPUT of driver -> damps oscillation. -> This is called SERIES TERMINATION. [SOLUTION] Driver --[R=33-100 ohm series]---- Cable ---- Load WHY SERIES RESISTOR? -> Adds resistance to the RLC. -> Increases damping ratio zeta = (R/2)*sqrt(C/L) toward 1. -> At zeta=1: critical damping -> ringing eliminated. VALUE CHOICE: -> Too small R: still underdamped (still rings). -> Too large R: overdamped (signal edges slow down). -> 33-100 ohm: practical sweet spot for most digital signals. [KEY INSIGHT] Long wires/cables ARE inductors and capacitors. Series R is the tool to add damping. This is called signal integrity engineering.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

A long cable is like a stretched rubber band with mass. When you pluck it (drive a fast digital edge), it vibrates at its natural frequency (the resonance of cable L and C). Without damping, it rings for many cycles. The series termination resistor is a piece of foam pressed against the rubber band: it absorbs the vibration energy and stops the oscillation after just one half-cycle. The key insight is that the ringing frequency depends on the cable geometry, not your circuit.

🔬 Physics

A cable with distributed inductance L_total and capacitance C_total forms an LC tank circuit. The natural resonant frequency is f_ring = 1/(2pi*sqrt(L*C)). When the driver transitions, it launches a wave that reflects from the unterminated load end and returns, causing constructive interference (ringing). The damping ratio zeta = R_total/(2*sqrt(L/C)) where R_total = R_driver + R_series. At zeta = 1 (critical damping), no overshoot occurs. R_series = Z_0 - Z_driver where Z_0 = sqrt(L/C) is the cable characteristic impedance.

📐 Math

Characteristic impedance: \( Z_0 = \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{1\mu\text{H}}{200\text{pF}}} = 70.7\,\Omega \)

Damping ratio: \( \zeta = \frac{R_{total}}{2} \sqrt{\frac{C}{L}} \)

Critical damping condition: \( R_{series} = Z_0 - Z_{driver\_out} \)

For 2m cable: L ≈ 1μH, C ≈ 200pF, f_ring = 1/(2π√(1μH × 200pF)) ≈ 356MHz

🔧 Practical

Source termination resistor: 33ohm 0402 1% placed within 2mm of driver output pin. For LVDS or differential signals, use 100ohm differential series resistors at the driver. For RS-485, use 120ohm parallel termination at the far end of the cable. Signal integrity rule of thumb: if cable length > wavelength/10 at the highest frequency of interest, termination is required. At 100MHz, wavelength in cable = 1.5m, so any cable longer than 15cm needs termination.

📖 Story

In 2017, the SpaceX Falcon 9 avionics team encountered RS-422 data corruption during vibration testing when harness cables flexed and their impedance changed. The original design had no series termination. Adding 100ohm source termination resistors on all 30 differential pairs eliminated the corruption. The root cause: cable capacitance varied from 80pF to 150pF as cables flexed, changing the reflection coefficient and causing intermittent bit errors only under vibration.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope with fast time base (1ns/div) on the load end of the cable. Step 2: Measure ringing frequency — confirms it is an LC resonance, not a circuit problem. Step 3: Add 33ohm series R at driver, observe if ringing decreases. Increase R until critically damped. Step 4: Verify logic levels are still valid after adding R (check V_OH and V_OL at load). Step 5: Use TDR (time domain reflectometry) to measure cable impedance directly.

🚀 Optimize

For very long cables or high data rates, use RS-485 or CAN bus transceivers designed for transmission-line driving. These have matched output impedance and built-in differential signaling for common-mode noise rejection. For single-ended signals beyond 1m, consider using LVDS (low-voltage differential signaling) which provides 3dB better noise margin and is immune to ground-loop noise.

Lec 11 — Scenario 6: Battery Drains Too Fast (Quiescent Current)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 6: Battery Drains Too Fast (Quiescent Current)

SCENARIO 6: "Battery-powered device drains battery in hours instead of weeks" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Device should run months on a battery. Actual runtime: a few hours. No obvious short circuit. Idle current measured: 50mA (should be <1mA). [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> Excess current drain in "idle" state. Q2: Time or frequency? -> Not time/frequency -- static DC current being wasted. Q3: Voltage or current? -> CURRENT problem. Too much current flowing. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Need to find WHERE current flows and LIMIT or BLOCK it. COMMON CAUSES: A) Voltage divider resistors too small -> constant current drain. B) Pull-up/pull-down resistors too small. C) LED indicator always on through small resistor. D) Capacitor with leakage. [SOLUTION] FIX A: Replace small R with LARGER values (100k instead of 1k). -> Same voltage ratio, 100x less current. FIX B: Use 100k pull-ups instead of 1k. -> 3.3V/100k = 33uA vs 3.3mA -> 100x saving. FIX C: Add series transistor switch for LED. -> LED only on when MCU drives GPIO HIGH. -> In sleep: GPIO LOW -> transistor OFF -> zero LED current. [KEY INSIGHT] In battery-powered design: EVERY resistor is a current drain. Rule: Use the LARGEST resistance that still works. Series switch (transistor) converts constant drain to controlled drain.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

Every resistor connected between VCC and GND is a leaky tap: it constantly drains the battery whether you need it or not. A 1k pull-up on a 3.3V rail draws 3.3mA all the time — that is 79mAh per day, killing a 500mAh battery in 6 days. The transistor switch for the LED is a gatekeeper: it only opens the drain pipe when commanded. The physical analogy is a faucet with a valve that is normally closed, versus a faucet that is always dripping.

🔬 Physics

Quiescent current I_Q = V_supply / R_total flows continuously through any resistor path from VCC to GND. Battery capacity depletes at rate I_Q. For a resistor divider with R1=R2=1k: I_Q = VCC/(R1+R2) = 3.3/2k = 1.65mA continuously, consuming 1.65mAh per hour. The transistor switch (BJT NPN: I_C = I_B × h_FE) completely cuts off current when I_B = 0 (GPIO LOW). With a 10k base resistor and GPIO HIGH: I_B = (3.3-0.7)/10k = 260uA; I_C = I_B × h_FE, sufficient to drive the LED.

📐 Math

Quiescent drain: \( I_Q = \frac{V_{CC}}{R} \)

Battery lifetime: \( t_{life} = \frac{C_{battery}}{I_{total}} \)

Pull-up comparison: \( 1k\Omega \to 3.3\,\text{mA} \) vs \( 100k\Omega \to 33\,\mu\text{A} \) (100× better)

Transistor on-state: \( I_{LED} = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{CE(sat)} - V_F}{R_{series}} \)

Transistor off-state: \( I_{LED} = 0 \) (I_{CE(off)} \approx \text{nA}) \)

🔧 Practical

Component choices for low-power design: 100kohm 0402 1% for pull-ups (33uA at 3.3V). Use P-channel MOSFET instead of NPN BJT for LED switching: zero gate current, lower V_DS(on). For voltage dividers: use 1Mohm resistors, accept higher noise susceptibility, add 100nF bypass cap to stabilize node against EMI. Target total idle current under 100uA for months-long battery life on AA batteries.

📖 Story

In 2022, the Tile location tracker engineering team discovered their v3 device draining 12mA in deep sleep (spec: 50uA). A current-by-elimination audit found three culprits: a 470-ohm LED resistor with always-on status LED (7mA), 4.7kohm pull-ups on I2C bus that was powered even in sleep (1.4mA), and a voltage divider on the battery voltage sense circuit (3.3mA). Changing to transistor-switched LED, enabling I2C power only when awake, and increasing divider resistors to 2Mohm reduced sleep current to 48uA.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Connect precision ammeter in series with battery. Measure idle current. Step 2: With MCU in deep sleep, systematically disconnect subsystems. Unplug LEDs, disconnect sensors, remove pull-up resistors one at a time. Current drops by X when you disconnect a component means that component draws X. Step 3: Measure each pull-up resistor: V_pullup / R = I_drain. Step 4: Check for partially-on logic gates: if digital output is floating or at intermediate voltage, the gate draws maximum supply current.

🚀 Optimize

Use the MCU's built-in pull-up resistors (typically 40kohm) instead of external components. Most modern MCUs allow disabling internal pull-ups in sleep mode. For sensors, use a MOSFET power switch to completely cut VCC to the sensor during sleep. The sensor then draws zero current, not even its "sleep mode" current of 1-10uA. This "power gating" technique can reduce total idle current by 10-100x on systems with multiple peripheral sensors.

Lec 11 — Scenario 7: RF Module Interference from Switching Supply

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 7: RF Module Interference from Switching Supply

SCENARIO 7: "Wireless receiver picks up interference from nearby switching supply" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] RF receiver (e.g., 433MHz module) working poorly. Lots of false triggers and missed signals. Problem disappears when switching power supply is turned off. Problem worse when supply load increases. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> Switching supply generates harmonics up to MHz range. -> These reach the RF module through power lines and radiation. Q2: Time or frequency? -> FREQUENCY problem. HF noise contaminating RF module. Q3: Voltage or current? -> BOTH: conducted noise (power lines) AND radiated noise (EM field). Q4: Series or parallel? -> Conducted path: inductor in SERIES to block HF. -> Radiated: capacitors in PARALLEL to absorb locally. [SOLUTION -- PI FILTER] Supply --[L=10uH]---- RF Module VCC | | C1=100nF C2=100nF | | GND GND Pi filter: C1 absorbs noise before it enters L. L blocks what C1 misses. C2 absorbs residual noise after L. FOR RADIATED NOISE: -> Keep RF module physically away from switching supply. -> Add grounded metal shield between them. -> Use star grounding. [KEY INSIGHT] Pi filter = C (parallel) + L (series) + C (parallel). Each component handles what the other misses. Always place EMI filter AT the entry point of the affected circuit.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The pi filter is a three-stage defense perimeter for your sensitive circuit. The first capacitor (C1) is the outer moat: noise currents from the supply prefer to flow into C1 (low impedance at HF) and short to GND before they even reach the inductor. The inductor L is the castle wall: it presents high impedance to any HF that made it past the moat, blocking it from reaching the circuit. The second capacitor (C2) is the inner moat: any residual HF that tunneled through the wall gets diverted to GND right at the circuit's doorstep.

🔬 Physics

The pi filter is a 3rd-order low-pass filter. Each capacitor contributes one pole and the inductor one more, giving a total rolloff of -60dB/decade above the corner frequency. For a single series inductor (no capacitors), conducted noise with a current path around the inductor (through stray capacitance of PCB traces) can bypass it. The two shunt capacitors eliminate these bypass paths by providing deliberate short-circuit paths to GND that dominate over the stray paths.

📐 Math

Pi filter corner: \( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{L \cdot C_{eq}}} \) where \( C_{eq} = C1 \parallel C2 \)

Attenuation slope above \( f_0 \): \( -60\,\text{dB/decade} \) (3rd-order)

Example: \( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{10\mu \times 50\text{n}}} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 22.4\mu} = 7.1\,\text{kHz} \)

Attenuation at 433MHz: \( \left(\frac{433M}{7.1k}\right)^3 \approx 2.2 \times 10^{11} = 213\,\text{dB} \)

🔧 Practical

Pi filter kit: L = 10uH (Murata LQM21FN100M00L) + C1=C2 = 100nF X7R 0402. Place C1 at the supply entry point to the board, C2 directly at the RF module VCC pin, L between them. Keep traces short (under 10mm). For 433MHz modules: add a copper pour ground plane under the module with vias every 5mm. Physical separation of switching supply and RF module: minimum 30mm, ideally on opposite sides of PCB.

📖 Story

In 2018, a Smart Home company recalled 50,000 units of a wireless temperature sensor because the 433MHz RF module had 40% packet loss when their product shared an outlet strip with a laptop charger. The charger's switching frequency was 65kHz; its 17th harmonic at 1.1MHz modulated the 433MHz receiver's AGC, desensitizing it. The fix was a pi filter (10uH + 100nF + 100nF) on the RF module power supply, plus PCB layout changes to move the RF module to the opposite corner from the boost converter.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Spectrum analyzer on RF module VCC (if available) or oscilloscope showing supply noise spectrum. Step 2: Identify switching frequency and harmonics. Step 3: Add pi filter, measure noise level again. Step 4: If radiated, wrap RF module in aluminum foil (temporary shield) and check if performance improves. If yes, permanent shield is needed. Step 5: Verify pi filter ground connections are solid (via to GND plane, not just trace).

🚀 Optimize

For production hardware, use a dedicated shielded inductor (drum core with integrated shield) to prevent the inductor itself from radiating. Consider a ferrite bead instead of inductor for frequencies above 10MHz: beads are optimized for HF EMI suppression, have lower DCR, and are physically smaller. Add an optional TVS diode from VCC to GND (PRTR5V0U2X) at the pi filter input for ESD protection.

Lec 11 — Scenario 8: MOSFET Destroyed by Inductive Kickback

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 8: MOSFET Destroyed by Inductive Kickback

SCENARIO 8: "Inductive motor drive keeps destroying the MOSFET transistor" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] MOSFET used to switch a DC motor ON and OFF. MOSFET keeps failing (short circuit failure) after a few hours. Replacement MOSFETs fail the same way. Circuit schematic seems correct. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> Motor coil is an INDUCTOR. -> When MOSFET turns OFF: inductor tries to maintain current. -> V = L*dI/dt -> enormous voltage spike at MOSFET drain. -> Spike exceeds MOSFET V_DS_max -> MOSFET avalanches -> fails. Q2: Time or frequency? -> TIME problem. Spike happens at the MOMENT of switch-off. Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem. Drain voltage spikes beyond ratings. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Flyback diode in PARALLEL with motor (clamps spike). -> RC snubber (R+C in series) in PARALLEL with MOSFET. [SOLUTION] LAYER 1: Flyback diode in parallel with motor (cathode to VCC) -> Clamps drain to VCC+0.7V when MOSFET turns OFF. LAYER 2: RC Snubber in parallel with MOSFET: MOSFET Drain --[R=10 series with C=100nF]-- GND -> C absorbs spike energy instantly. -> R limits discharge current (prevents C short-circuiting spike). [KEY INSIGHT] RC snubber = R in SERIES with C, placed in PARALLEL with the device. R+C in series = snubber (absorbs energy, R limits current). C alone in parallel = dangerous (no current limiting). The SERIES R is critical -- never use C alone as a snubber.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

When you switch off a motor, the inductor is like a hydraulic pump that cannot stop instantly. The water (current) must keep flowing somewhere. Without a flyback diode, the only path is backward through the MOSFET, but the drain voltage rockets skyward at V = L × dI/dt to force the current through. The flyback diode is an emergency drain pipe opened exactly when needed: it provides a free path for the inductive current to circulate back through the motor, clamping the voltage to just VCC + 0.7V. The RC snubber is a secondary shock absorber for the fast initial spike that the diode is too slow to catch.

🔬 Physics

The inductor stores energy E = 0.5LI^2. When the circuit opens, this energy must go somewhere. If no path exists, the voltage rises until the device breaks down (V = L × dI/dt, where dt approaches zero as the circuit opens, driving V to infinity). The flyback diode (Schottky preferred: 20ns recovery vs 200ns for silicon) provides a conduction path when V_D > V_CC. The remaining energy dissipates in the motor's DC resistance and diode forward voltage. The RC snubber handles the leading-edge transient (the first nanoseconds of turn-off) where the flyback diode has not yet fully recovered.

📐 Math

Inductive energy: \( E = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 \)

Unclamped spike: \( V_{spike} = L \cdot \frac{dI}{dt} \to \infty \) as circuit opens

With flyback diode: \( V_{drain} = V_{CC} + V_F = V_{CC} + 0.7\,\text{V} \)

Snubber R value: \( R_s = \sqrt{\frac{L}{C_s}} \) (critical damping of snubber)

Snubber C value: \( C_s = \frac{I_0^2}{V_{clamp}^2} \cdot L \) (limits peak voltage to V_clamp)

🔧 Practical

Flyback diode: Schottky type, e.g., B340A (40V, 3A), placed physically as close as possible to the motor terminals (within 20mm). RC snubber: R = 10ohm 1W wirewound + C = 100nF 50V film capacitor (not ceramic — ceramic can crack under current pulses). For PWM motor drives: the flyback diode must be rated for average current (not just peak). At 50% duty cycle and 2A motor current: average diode current = 1A.

📖 Story

In 2016, a Makita cordless drill engineering team traced premature MOSFET failures in their first lithium-ion brushed motor drive to missing flyback diodes in an attempted cost-reduction redesign. The original design with 1N5819 Schottky diodes had zero field failures in 500,000 units. After removing the diodes (saving $0.08 per unit), the MOSFETs failed within 200 motor start cycles at low temperatures when inrush current was highest. Re-adding the diodes, plus RC snubbers, achieved zero MOSFET failures in 2 million cycles.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope with 200V/div range on MOSFET drain during turn-off. Look for voltage spike above VCC. Step 2: If spike exists and exceeds V_DS_max, add flyback diode immediately. Step 3: After diode, check for residual fast spike (sub-microsecond) still reaching V_DS_max. Step 4: If fast spike exists, add RC snubber. Adjust R to balance speed vs attenuation. Step 5: Run thermal imaging to verify snubber R is not overheating (check power P = 0.5 × C × V^2 × f per cycle).

🚀 Optimize

Use a Schottky diode (lower forward voltage = lower clamping level = more energy in diode = faster dissipation) instead of silicon for motors running above 10kHz PWM. For very high power motors (kW range), use an active clamp circuit: a Zener diode or TVS in series with a diode, which clamps the drain voltage to a fixed voltage and actively dissipates the inductive energy. The active clamp allows faster current fall time than a flyback diode, reducing switching losses.

Lec 11 — Scenario 9: Speaker Distortion (Zobel Network)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 9: Speaker Distortion (Zobel Network)

SCENARIO 9: "Speaker produces distorted sound at high volume on bass frequencies" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Audio amplifier driving an 8-ohm speaker. At moderate volume: clean sound. At high volume on bass (50-200Hz): distortion, "flapping" sound. Oscilloscope shows clipping on amplifier output. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> At bass: speaker draws very high current. -> Speaker impedance drops at bass resonance. -> Amplifier cannot supply enough current -> clips -> distortion. Q2: Time or frequency? -> FREQUENCY problem. Specific frequency range (bass) causing issue. Q3: Voltage or current? -> CURRENT problem. Amplifier current-limited at bass. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Zobel network: R+C in SERIES, placed in PARALLEL with speaker. -> Compensates for rising impedance at HF. -> Flattens total load impedance across frequency range. [SOLUTION -- ZOBEL NETWORK] Amplifier ---- Speaker (8 ohm) | [R=8 ohm + C=1uF in series] -- in PARALLEL with speaker | GND R = speaker nominal impedance C = L_coil / R^2 (for speaker coil inductance) [KEY INSIGHT] Series RC (as a unit) placed in PARALLEL = frequency-dependent load. Low frequencies: C is high impedance -> combination ignored. High frequencies: C becomes small -> R dominates -> controlled loading. This is the Zobel network -- used in every professional speaker crossover.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The speaker's voice coil is an inductor: its impedance rises with frequency as Z = sqrt(R^2 + (wL)^2). Without the Zobel, the amplifier sees a load that jumps from 8ohm at midrange to 30ohm+ at 20kHz. The Zobel network is an impedance equalizer: at low frequencies, the series capacitor is a high-impedance roadblock, so essentially no current flows through the Zobel branch. At high frequencies, the capacitor becomes a wire, and current flows through the 8ohm Zobel resistor, exactly compensating for the speaker's rising coil impedance.

🔬 Physics

The speaker's total impedance is Z_spk = sqrt(R_voice^2 + (2pifL_coil)^2). The Zobel network impedance is Z_z = R_z + 1/(j2pifc) = R_z - j/(2pifc). When placed in parallel, the total impedance magnitude becomes flatter. At the crossover frequency f_z = R_z/(2piL_coil), the Zobel begins actively compensating. The amplifier's output stage sees a resistive load at all frequencies, operating in its most efficient and lowest-distortion region.

📐 Math

Speaker impedance: \( Z_{spk}(f) = \sqrt{R_{voice}^2 + (2\pi f L_{coil})^2} \)

Zobel design: \( R_z = R_{nominal} \), \( C_z = \frac{L_{coil}}{R_{nominal}^2} \)

For 8Ω speaker with L_coil = 0.5mH: \( C_z = 0.5\text{mH}/64 = 7.8\,\mu\text{F} \)

At crossover frequency: \( f_z = \frac{R_z}{2\pi L_{coil}} = \frac{8}{2\pi \times 0.5\text{mH}} = 2.5\,\text{kHz} \)

🔧 Practical

Measure speaker coil inductance with LCR meter at 1kHz: L_coil = measured inductance. Calculate C_z = L_coil / R_nominal^2. Use 1% tolerance resistor matched to measured DC resistance of speaker. Capacitor: MKT or MKP film type (not electrolytic — too high ESR and non-linearity). Typical values for 8ohm speaker: R_z = 8ohm, C_z = 4.7-10uF. Wattage of R_z: P_R = V_out^2 / (2 × R_z) at maximum power, use 5W minimum.

📖 Story

In 2020, the Klipsch Reference speaker engineering team upgraded their professional studio monitor series after noticing that their Class D amplifier module clipped asymmetrically at high volumes on test tones above 10kHz. Measurement revealed that without a Zobel network, the speaker's impedance at 20kHz reached 52 ohm (vs 8 ohm nominal), causing the amplifier's output current limiter to engage much earlier on the treble-heavy content. Adding a Zobel (8ohm + 10uF per channel) eliminated the clipping and improved maximum SPL by 3dB.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Measure speaker impedance vs frequency with an impedance analyzer or DIY measurement (signal generator + resistor divider). Confirm the rising impedance curve. Step 2: Calculate Zobel values from measured L_coil. Step 3: Build Zobel on prototype PCB. Step 4: Re-measure impedance with Zobel in parallel: should be flatter (within 2ohm from 20Hz to 20kHz). Step 5: Listen test at maximum volume: distortion should be reduced at high frequencies. Step 6: Verify Zobel R is not overheating (thermal camera or thermometer).

🚀 Optimize

For professional-grade crossover networks, add both a Zobel network (compensates coil inductance) and a notch filter at the bass resonance frequency (compensates the impedance peak at fs). The notch filter is an RLC circuit in parallel with the woofer that absorbs excess current at the resonance peak. Together these techniques give professional audio amplifiers a nearly flat 8ohm load at all frequencies, maximizing power transfer efficiency.

Lec 11 — Scenario 10: Voltage Reference Temperature Drift

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 11 — Scenario 10: Voltage Reference Temperature Drift

SCENARIO 10: "Precision voltage reference drifts when temperature changes" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Circuit uses R divider to create 1.65V reference from 3.3V. R1=R2=10k. Works at room temperature. In hot environment (60C): reference drifts to 1.72V. ADC readings become inaccurate. [THINKING PROCESS] Q1: What is wrong? -> Resistors have TEMPERATURE COEFFICIENT (tempco). -> If R1 and R2 do not change equally: ratio shifts -> Vref drifts. Q2: Time or frequency? -> STABILITY problem (slow drift with temperature). Q3: Voltage or current? -> VOLTAGE problem. Reference voltage is unstable. Q4: Series or parallel? -> Capacitor in PARALLEL with R2: filters fast noise. -> But for temperature drift: the real fix is better components. [SOLUTION -- MULTI-LEVEL] LEVEL 1: Use 1%, 25ppm/C matched resistors (same batch). LEVEL 2: Add C=10uF in parallel with R2. LEVEL 3: Add op-amp BUFFER between divider and ADC. LEVEL 4: Replace divider with dedicated Vref IC (e.g., LM4040 1.65V, 50ppm/C). [KEY INSIGHT] Parallel C = noise filter + transient absorber. C does NOT fix temperature drift -- only better components fix drift. Engineers must distinguish: "Is this a component quality issue or a circuit topology issue?"

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

A resistor divider is a precise ratio that depends on both resistors changing equally with temperature. If they drift independently (even 10% differently), the ratio shifts and the reference voltage drifts. The capacitor in parallel is a stabilizer for fast noise, like a sponge absorbing rapid pressure changes; but it cannot fix a slow, steady drift caused by resistor tempco mismatch — just as a sponge cannot stop a slow, steady leak caused by a corroded pipe. The op-amp buffer removes loading effects by presenting infinite input impedance, so the reference does not sag when the ADC draws current.

🔬 Physics

A resistor's value follows R(T) = R_0 × (1 + TC × (T - T_0)) where TC is the temperature coefficient in ppm/C. If R1 and R2 have different TCs (TC1 ≠ TC2), the ratio V_out/V_in = R2/(R1+R2) changes with temperature. Matched resistors from the same reel have nearly identical TCs because they are made from the same material batch, so temperature changes affect both equally, preserving the ratio. The parallel capacitor removes fast noise by providing a low-impedance shunt path at high frequencies, but at DC (where temperature drift occurs), it is an open circuit and has no effect.

📐 Math

Voltage divider: \( V_{ref} = V_{CC} \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \)

Drift due to TC mismatch: \( \Delta V_{ref} \approx V_{CC} \cdot \frac{\Delta TC \cdot \Delta T}{4} \)

Example: TC mismatch = 50ppm/C, delta_T = 35C: \( \Delta V_{ref} = 3.3 \times \frac{50\text{e-6} \times 35}{4} = 1.44\,\text{mV} \)

For ADC: 12-bit at 3.3V range: 1 LSB = 0.8mV. Drift of 1.44mV = 1.8 LSB — unacceptable for precision.

🔧 Practical

Matched resistors: order 0.1% 25ppm/C from same batch (Vishay TNPW series 0402, 0.1%, 25ppm matched). Capacitor: 10uF X5R 0805 + 100nF X7R 0402 in parallel. Op-amp buffer: OPA333 (zero-drift, 0.1uV/C offset drift, MSOP-8). For highest precision: LM4040-1.65 voltage reference (1.65V, 50ppm/C, SOT-23-3), which avoids resistor matching entirely and provides 0.1% initial accuracy.

📖 Story

In 2021, the Fluke calibration laboratory team found that a new batch of their voltage calibrator reference boards showed 0.05% drift over their −20C to +70C operating range, exceeding their 0.02% specification. The root cause: two 0.1% tolerance resistors in the reference divider had been substituted with parts from a different manufacturer during a component shortage. The new parts had 100ppm/C TC vs the specified 25ppm/C, and the two parts came from different reels with a TC mismatch of 30ppm/C. Replacing with matched 25ppm/C parts from a single reel restored the 0.02% specification.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Monitor reference voltage with precise DMM as temperature increases from 25C to 60C. Plot V_ref vs temperature. Step 2: Calculate measured TC: (V_60 - V_25) / (V_25 × 35C) in ppm/C. Step 3: Disconnect load from reference divider: if drift changes, loading is contributing. Step 4: Add 10uF cap in parallel with lower resistor: if high-frequency noise decreases, filtering is helping. Step 5: Measure both resistors with LCR meter at low frequency to confirm they match within 0.1%.

🚀 Optimize

For the ultimate precision reference in production, use a bandgap voltage reference IC (e.g., ADR421: 2.5V, 3ppm/C, 0.04% initial accuracy) combined with trimming resistors or a DAC for fine calibration. The bandgap reference exploits the PTAT (proportional to absolute temperature) and CTAT (complementary to absolute temperature) characteristics of bipolar transistors, which cancel each other to produce a voltage nearly independent of temperature.

Lec 12 — Scenario 11: RS-232 Long Cable Corruption

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 11: RS-232 Long Cable Corruption

SCENARIO 11: "RS-232 communication line picks up noise over 10-meter cable" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] MCU communicates with PC via RS-232 over a 10m cable. At 9600 baud: works fine. At 115200 baud: random data corruption, garbage characters. Shorter cable (1m) at 115200 baud: works fine. [DIAGNOSIS] Q1: Long cable + high speed = signal corruption. Q2: FREQUENCY problem. Higher baud = higher frequency transitions. At 115200 baud, bit period = 8.7us. Long cable has capacitance (~100pF/m x 10m = 1nF). RC slows transitions -> bits smear into each other (ISI). Q3: VOLTAGE problem. Signal edges degrade. Q4: Need impedance matching. Series resistor at transmitter end. Q5: Digital signal (AC transitions). Q6: Must respond within 1 bit period = 8.7us. [SOLUTION] TX --[R=100 series]---- 10m cable ---- RX | C=100pF (cable cap) Series R at transmitter: -> Matches cable impedance (100-120 ohm for RS-232). -> Damps reflections from cable end. At receiver: add RC low-pass filter: RX --[R=100]---- MCU RX pin | C=100pF to GND f_c = 1/(2pi*100*100p) = 16MHz -> passes 115kHz, blocks cable noise. [KEY INSIGHT] Long cables are transmission lines with Z_0 (characteristic impedance). Series R at driver = source termination = match Z_out to Z_0. This eliminates reflections. Rule: R_series ~= Z_0 - Z_driver_output.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

A 10m cable is not a wire — it is a distributed RC transmission line. At 9600 baud, each bit lasts 104 microseconds: the cable's RC time constant (1ns/foot × 33ft) of about 330ns is negligible. At 115200 baud, each bit lasts only 8.7 microseconds, and the cable has 1nF of capacitance that must be charged and discharged through the driver impedance. The signal edges smear: instead of sharp transitions, you get exponential curves. Adjacent bits blur into each other (inter-symbol interference, ISI), causing read errors.

🔬 Physics

The cable's distributed capacitance C_cable = 100pF/m × 10m = 1nF and inductance L_cable = 500nH/m × 10m = 5uH give a characteristic impedance Z_0 = sqrt(L/C) = sqrt(5u/1n) = 70.7 ohm. When the driver (source impedance ~10ohm) drives this cable, the impedance mismatch causes reflections. A wave launched at the transmitter, reaching the open-circuit receiver end, reflects and returns toward the transmitter. This reflection adds to or subtracts from the original signal, causing ringing and ISI.

📐 Math

Cable RC time constant: \( \tau = R_{driver} \cdot C_{cable} \)

ISI condition: \( \tau > 0.1 \times T_{bit} \) causes errors

At 115200 baud: \( T_{bit} = 8.7\,\mu\text{s} \), \( \tau_{cable} = 10\Omega \times 1\text{nF} = 10\text{ns} \ll T_{bit} \) — OK

But reflections add: \( V_{reflection} = \Gamma \cdot V_{incident} \) where \( \Gamma = (Z_L - Z_0)/(Z_L + Z_0) \)

Source termination: \( R_s = Z_0 - Z_{driver} = 70.7 - 10 \approx 60\Omega \)

🔧 Practical

Series termination: 68ohm 0402 1% at TX output (adjust to 33-100ohm for best eye diagram). Receiver filter: 100ohm 0402 + 100pF C0G 0402 at each RX input (place within 5mm of MCU pin). For RS-232 (bipolar +/-12V levels), use MAX232 or equivalent line driver/receiver that handles the full ±15V common-mode range. If signal integrity is critical, consider RS-485 (differential) which inherently rejects common-mode noise and supports much longer cables.

📖 Story

In 2019, a Siemens factory automation team installed a new CNC machine controller communicating with legacy RS-232 PLCs via 15m cable runs through the factory floor. At 115200 baud (required for a real-time motion profile), they had 2-3% packet error rates. Adding 100ohm series resistors at each transmitter output and 100pF input filters at each receiver reduced the error rate to below 0.001%, meeting their specification.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope with 1Gs/s sample rate on receiver input. Zoom in at 1us/div. Look for ringing (indicates impedance mismatch) or slow edges (indicates RC loading). Step 2: Measure rise time (10%-90%). If rise time > 0.7 x bit period, signal will be unreliable. Step 3: Add series R at transmitter in 10ohm increments. Measure eye diagram improvement at each step. Step 4: Verify receiver threshold levels are met despite slower edges.

🚀 Optimize

Upgrade to RS-485 differential signaling: the SMPTE standard for the same wiring (twisted pair). RS-485 supports 1200m cables at 100kbps or 100m at 10Mbps, compared to RS-232's maximum of 15m at 20kbps by specification. The differential signaling provides 6dB better noise margin and rejects ground-loop noise between equipment. Transceivers: MAX485 (5V, half-duplex) or MAX3485 (3.3V, half-duplex).

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 13: Gate Driver Overheating at High Frequency

SCENARIO 13: "MOSFET gate driver heats up severely during high-frequency switching" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] MOSFET gate driver IC runs hot at 100kHz switching. At 10kHz: driver is cool. Energy per cycle: E = 0.5 * C * V^2 = 0.5 * 10n * 100 = 500nJ. At 100kHz: P = E * f = 500n * 100k = 50mW per MOSFET. [SOLUTION -- GATE RESISTOR] Driver --[R_g = 10-33 ohm series]---- MOSFET Gate Series R_g at gate: -> Limits peak gate current: I_peak = V_drive / R_g = 10V/10 = 1A -> Damps gate oscillation (gate + PCB trace = LC tank). -> Reduces di/dt -> reduces EMI generated by switching. 💡 Intuition

The MOSFET gate is a capacitor. Every time you switch the MOSFET on, you charge this capacitor from the driver (energy stored = 0.5CV^2). Every time you switch off, you discharge it (energy dissipated in the driver's pull-down transistor). Neither the MOSFET nor the load gets this energy — it is all burned in the gate driver. The gate resistor limits the peak current (like a speed governor on the charging/discharging process), spreading the energy over a slightly longer time, which allows the driver to manage its thermal budget without overheating.

🔬 Physics

MOSFET gate capacitance C_iss (input capacitance) is typically 1-100nF depending on the device. Charging it to V_GS = 10V stores E = 0.5 × C_iss × V_GS^2. This energy is sourced from the gate driver supply and must be re-released on every turn-off cycle. Total gate drive power: P_gate = C_iss × V_GS^2 × f_sw. At 100kHz with C_iss = 10nF: P_gate = 10n × 100 × 100k = 100mW. With 10 parallel MOSFETs, this reaches 1W — requiring the gate driver to have 1W+ thermal dissipation capability.

📐 Math

Gate energy per cycle: \( E_{gate} = \frac{1}{2} C_{iss} V_{GS}^2 \)

Gate drive power: \( P_{gate} = C_{iss} \cdot V_{GS}^2 \cdot f_{sw} \)

Example: \( P = 10\text{nF} \times (10\text{V})^2 \times 100\text{kHz} = 100\,\text{mW} \)

Peak gate current: \( I_{peak} = \frac{V_{drive}}{R_g} = \frac{10V}{22\Omega} = 455\,\text{mA} \)

Switching time: \( t_{sw} \approx 2.2 \times R_g \times C_{iss} \)

🔧 Practical

💡 Intuition

🔬 Physics

An instrumentation amplifier with 100V/V gain amplifies this 1mV bounce to 100mV — 10% of a full-scale 1V signal. Star grounding eliminates this by ensuring that digital return currents flow back to the supply through the digital GND path and never share impedance with the analog GND path.

Ground bounce: \( V_{bounce} = L_{gnd} \cdot \frac{dI_{digital}}{dt} \)

Lec 12 — Scenario 15: Op-Amp Oscillation (Stability)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 15: Op-Amp Oscillation (Stability)

SCENARIO 15: "Op-amp output oscillates even with no input signal applied" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Op-amp configured as non-inverting amplifier (gain=10). Input: tied to GND through 10k. Output: oscillating at ~1MHz square wave. Removing the gain (unity gain follower): stops oscillation. High-gain configuration: always oscillates. [DIAGNOSIS] Q1: Amplifier oscillating = positive feedback condition. Q2: FREQUENCY problem. 1MHz where phase shift = 180 degrees. Q3: VOLTAGE problem. Output feeding back in wrong phase. Q4: Compensation capacitor reduces high-frequency gain. Q5: AC problem. DC operation fine. Q6: Must remain stable up to bandwidth of op-amp. [SOLUTION] Option A -- Feedback capacitor (most common): Place C=10pF in PARALLEL with the feedback resistor R_f. -> At high frequency: C shorts out R_f -> gain drops to 1. -> Before phase reaches 180 degrees: gain < 1 -> no oscillation. Option B -- Input filter: INV_IN --[R=1k]-- node | C=100pF to GND Option C -- Supply decoupling (often the real fix): -> Add 100nF ceramic cap from EACH supply pin (V+ and V-) to GND. -> Place physically AT the op-amp package (within 5mm). -> Without decoupling: supply droop -> op-amp amplifies it -> oscillates. [KEY INSIGHT] Op-amp oscillation = stability problem = phase margin < 45 degrees. Parallel C across feedback R = dominant pole compensation. Rule: Every high-gain op-amp needs supply decoupling AND may need feedback cap.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

An op-amp is designed to correct errors: if the output is lower than commanded, it increases. This is negative feedback at DC. But at high frequencies, the signal takes time to travel through the op-amp's internal stages, accumulating phase delay. At 180 degrees of phase delay, the negative feedback becomes positive feedback: a small perturbation is amplified and reinforced rather than corrected. The compensation capacitor across the feedback resistor is a deliberate speed governor: it makes the op-amp's gain drop to 1 before the phase reaches 180 degrees, removing the possibility of self-reinforcing oscillation.

🔬 Physics

The op-amp has an open-loop gain A(f) that rolls off at 20dB/decade above the first pole frequency (typically a few Hz for a unity-gain-stable op-amp). The phase of A(f) reaches -90 degrees at the first pole and continues to -180 degrees beyond the second pole. The closed-loop gain G = 1 + R_f/R_in. If G > A(f) at the frequency where phase = -180 degrees, the system oscillates. The compensation capacitor C_f places a zero in the feedback network at f_z = 1/(2pi*R_f*C_f), which causes the closed-loop gain to roll off early, maintaining phase margin.

📐 Math

Phase margin requirement: \( PM > 45^\circ \) for stable operation

Compensation pole: \( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi R_f C_f} = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 9k \times 10p} = 1.77\,\text{MHz} \)

Closed-loop bandwidth without C_f: \( f_{BW} = GBW/G = 10\text{MHz}/10 = 1\,\text{MHz} \)

Phase at 1MHz (2nd pole effect): phase can approach -180° — oscillation!

With C_f: gain rolls off at f_c, phase recovers above f_c — stable

🔧 Practical

For high-gain op-amp circuits: place 10-100pF C0G 0402 in parallel with R_f (adjust value until oscillation stops). Always decouple both supply pins with 100nF X7R 0402 physically within 5mm of each V+ and V- pin. For capacitive loads (driving cables or ADC inputs): add 100ohm series isolation resistor at op-amp output to prevent the load capacitance from adding phase shift in the feedback loop. Op-amp selection: choose unity-gain stable types (OPA2134, LM358) or use decompensated types (OPA637) only in high-gain circuits.

The large photodiode capacitance created a resonance with the feedback resistor, causing oscillation at 5MHz. Adding a 2.2pF feedback capacitor (Cf = Cd/(2*pi*Rf*Cd*GBW)) in parallel with the 1Mohm feedback resistor provided 45 degrees of phase margin and completely eliminated the oscillation.

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 16: Solenoid Valve Weak at PWM

[SOLUTION -- PEAK-AND-HOLD DRIVE] OPEN phase (t=0 to t=5ms): Full 12V, no PWM. -> Current builds to max: I = 12/50 = 240mA. -> Solenoid fully opens. Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The solenoid coil is an inductor. Current through an inductor does not jump instantly — it builds up exponentially with time constant tau = L/R. If you switch the power on and off faster than it takes the current to build up, the current (and therefore the magnetic force) never reaches its maximum. It is like trying to fill a bathtub by opening and closing the tap faster than the water can flow in. The peak-and-hold technique is the elegant solution: fill the tub fully first (peak phase), then partially close the tap to just maintain the level (hold phase).

🔬 Physics

The inductor current follows I(t) = I_max × (1 - e^(-t/tau)) during the ON phase, where tau = L/R = 500mH/50ohm = 10ms and I_max = V/R = 12/50 = 240mA. At t = 0.5tau (5ms for 50% duty at 100Hz PWM), I(0.5tau) = 240mA × (1 - e^(-0.5)) = 240mA × 0.39 = 94mA. Solenoid holding force scales as I^2: at 39% of maximum current, force is only 15% of maximum. The solenoid appears "weak" because the PWM period is comparable to tau.

📐 Math

Inductor time constant: \( \tau = \frac{L}{R} = \frac{500\text{mH}}{50\Omega} = 10\,\text{ms} \)

Current at turn-on: \( I(t) = I_{max}(1 - e^{-t/\tau}) \)

At 50% duty, 100Hz: \( I(5\text{ms}) = 240\text{mA} \times (1-e^{-0.5}) = 94\,\text{mA} \) (39% of max)

Force \( \propto I^2 \): at 39% current, force = 15% of maximum!

Rule: \( t_{ON} \geq 5\tau \) to reach 99% of I_max

🔧 Practical

Peak-and-hold circuit: SW1 = IRLZ34N MOSFET (full drive), SW2 = IRF540N MOSFET + 150ohm 2W resistor (hold mode). Control: MCU timer drives SW1 for 50ms (5tau) at startup, then switches to SW2. C) Add input filter capacitor (parallel, low-leakage film type): Signal --[R=1k]---- INA input | C=10nF (film, NOT electrolytic) | GND [KEY INSIGHT] PCB surface resistance is a REAL component at pA-nA current levels. Slow drift = very long RC time constants from unexpected sources. Solution: Guard rings, lower impedance, film capacitors only.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

At nanoampere current levels, the PCB itself becomes a resistor. A freshly cleaned PCB surface between two traces has a leakage resistance of perhaps 10 gigaohms. The instrumentation amplifier's bias current (10nA) charges the stray capacitance at the input node through this leakage resistance. With tau = 10Gohm × 1pF = 10 seconds, each time constant adds only a small charge, but over 30 minutes (180 time constants), the voltage builds to its final value. The guard ring is the elegant solution: it surrounds the signal trace with a ring driven to the same potential, so there is zero voltage difference between the guard and the signal — zero leakage current, zero drift.

🔬 Physics

The instrumentation amplifier's bias current I_B flows into the input node (or out, depending on polarity). If the input node has finite impedance Z_node = R_source || (1/(jωC_stray)), the bias current charges the stray capacitance through the source resistance, creating a voltage V = I_B × R_source at DC. But with PCB surface leakage, additional paths appear: I_leak = V_signal / R_PCB. This leakage current charges node capacitance at rate dV/dt = I_leak / C_stray, causing drift with tau = R_PCB × C_stray. The guard ring eliminates I_leak by maintaining the leakage current source terminal at the same potential as the destination.

📐 Math

PCB leakage RC: \( \tau_{drift} = R_{PCB} \cdot C_{trace} = 10\,\text{G}\Omega \times 1\,\text{pF} = 10\,\text{s} \)

Bias current offset: \( V_{offset} = I_{bias} \times R_{source} = 10\text{nA} \times 100\text{k} = 1\,\text{mV} \)

Guard ring condition: \( V_{guard} = V_{signal} \Rightarrow I_{leakage} = 0 \)

Drift rate without guard: \( dV/dt = I_{bias}/C_{stray} = 10\text{nA}/1\text{pF} = 10\,\text{mV/s} \)

🔧 Practical

Guard ring: PCB trace surrounding the high-impedance signal traces, connected to the INA's reference input or to the signal node through a unity-gain buffer. Route signal traces as short as possible (<5mm). Use Teflon (PTFE) board material for the most critical sections (R_surface > 10^15 ohm vs FR4's 10^10 ohm). Low-leakage film capacitor (Wima FKP2, <1pA leakage at 25C). Electrolytic capacitors have 100-1000pA leakage — never use them in high-impedance circuits.

📖 Story

In 2020, the Mettler-Toledo precision balance laboratory (Columbus, Ohio) experienced unexpected drift in their XPE205 analytical balance (readability: 0.01mg) after a factory reflow oven was installed nearby. The oven's electromagnetic field induced a surface charge on the PCB insulating layers near the high-impedance weigh cell amplifier input. The drift was 0.3mg over 45 minutes. Solution: replacing the standard FR4 substrate in the amplifier section with Rogers 4350B (low-loss, high-surface-resistance material) and adding a guard ring reduced drift to below 0.005mg.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Short the INA input (disconnect load cell). If drift stops, the load cell or its wiring is the source. Step 2: Remove the PCB from its enclosure. If drift changes, humidity or surface contamination is a factor. Step 3: Apply insulating tape over the PCB surface between the input traces. Does drift decrease? If yes, surface leakage is the problem. Step 4: Measure bias current: V_OS vs V_OS with input resistors swapped (if bias current is positive, one configuration will show positive drift, the other negative). Lec 12 — Scenario 18: CAN Bus Failure in Automotive (Ignition Noise)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 18: CAN Bus Failure in Automotive (Ignition Noise)

SCENARIO 18: "CAN bus communication fails intermittently in automotive environment" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] CAN bus network in a car (multiple ECUs). Works perfectly in workshop. Fails intermittently when engine is running. Failures correlate with ignition firing, alternator load changes. CAN bus uses twisted pair cable. [DIAGNOSIS] Q1: Engine ignition = huge electromagnetic interference source. Alternator = AC machine generating switching transients. These couple into CAN bus as common-mode noise. Q2: FREQUENCY problem. Ignition spike = broadband EMI (DC to MHz). Q3: VOLTAGE problem. Common-mode voltage on CAN bus lines. Q4: Need to REJECT common-mode noise. CAN is differential -- inherently rejects common-mode. But if common-mode voltage exceeds transceiver range: fails. [SOLUTION -- MULTI-LAYER] LAYER 1 -- Common-mode choke (SERIES on both lines): CAN_H --[L_cm]---- CAN_H out CAN_L --[L_cm]---- CAN_L out (Both windings on same core, opposing directions) -> Differential CAN signal passes freely (fields cancel). -> Common-mode noise blocked (fields add in core). LAYER 2 -- TVS diodes (PARALLEL for spike protection): CAN_H --[TVS]-- GND CAN_L --[TVS]-- GND -> Clamps any spike above +/-12V to GND. LAYER 3 -- Split termination with capacitor: 120 ohm split: 60 ohm + C=4.7nF + 60 ohm (midpoint cap to GND) -> Cap provides low-impedance path for HF common-mode noise to GND. [KEY INSIGHT] Common-mode choke = dual inductor that only blocks noise present on BOTH lines equally. Passes differential signals perfectly. Standard solution for any differential bus (CAN, RS-485, Ethernet) in electrically noisy environments.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

CAN bus is inherently immune to differential noise: any noise that appears equally on both CAN_H and CAN_L wires (common-mode noise) is cancelled when the receiver subtracts them. But ignition pulses and alternator ripple are common-mode transients that can reach hundreds of volts. The common-mode choke is an elegant filter that passes the differential CAN signal (the two wire currents flow in opposite directions, so their magnetic fields cancel inside the choke core) while blocking common-mode noise (both wires carry the same noise current in the same direction, fields add, high impedance blocks them).

🔬 Physics

The common-mode choke has two windings on a ferrite core, wound in opposing directions. For differential signals (I_H = -I_L), the magnetic fluxes from both windings cancel, resulting in zero net inductance — the choke appears invisible to differential signals. For common-mode noise (I_H = I_L = I_noise), the magnetic fluxes add, creating high inductance Z_cm = jωL_cm, which blocks the noise. The TVS diodes clamp the voltage on each wire independently to prevent the transceiver IC from experiencing voltages beyond its ±12V or ±36V (automotive) rating.

📐 Math

Common-mode impedance: \( Z_{cm} = j\omega L_{cm} \) (both windings in series for CM)

Differential impedance: \( Z_{diff} \approx 0 \) (fields cancel for DM signal)

TVS clamp voltage: \( V_{clamp} = V_{BR} + I_{pk} \times R_{dyn} \)

CAN standard termination: \( R_T = 120\,\Omega \) (two 60+60 for split)

Split cap cutoff: \( f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi \times 60\Omega \times 4.7\text{nF}} = 565\,\text{kHz} \) (blocks HF CM above 565kHz)

🔧 Practical

Common-mode choke: Wurth Elektronik 744272470 (47uH, CAN-optimized, SMD). TVS diodes: SMBJ12CA (bidirectional, 12V clamp, 600W peak power, DO-214AA) on CAN_H and CAN_L to GND. Split termination: 2×60ohm + 4.7nF (TDK C1608C0G1H472J) between them. CAN transceiver: TJA1044 (NXP, automotive grade, ±58V fault protection, AEC-Q100). Layout: place TVS closest to the connector, then choke, then termination.

📖 Story

In 2019, a Tier-1 automotive supplier delivering Body Control Modules (BCM) for a European OEM experienced CAN bus communication failures in 0.1% of vehicles during recall inspection. Root cause: a batch of BCMs had CM chokes with 20% lower CM impedance than specified, reducing ignition spike rejection from 40dB to 28dB. Lec 12 — Scenario 19: Buck Converter Output Ripple Reduction

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 19: Buck Converter Output Ripple Reduction

SCENARIO 19: "Buck converter output voltage has 50mV ripple -- spec says < 5mV" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Buck converter: 12V input -> 5V output, 2A load. Switching frequency: 400kHz. Output capacitor: 100uF electrolytic. Ripple measured: 50mV peak-to-peak. Specification: < 5mV ripple. Need 10x improvement. [DIAGNOSIS] Q1: Ripple too high on output. Q2: FREQUENCY problem. 400kHz ripple on DC output. Q3: VOLTAGE problem. Q4: Two approaches: A) Increase output capacitance (parallel more caps). B) Add additional LC filter stage (series L, parallel C). [SOLUTION -- POST-FILTER LC STAGE (BETTER)] Buck output --[L=4.7uH]---- Clean output | C=10uF (ceramic, low ESR) | GND Attenuation at 400kHz: f_0 = 1/(2pi*sqrt(4.7u*10u)) = 733Hz. Attenuation = (f_0/f_noise)^2 = (733/400k)^2 = 0.0003% Practical: >40dB reduction -> 50mV -> 0.5mV (below 5mV spec!) WHY CERAMIC NOT ELECTROLYTIC for post-filter? -> Electrolytic ESR=0.1-1 ohm -> at 400kHz: significant voltage drop. -> Ceramic: ESR < 0.01 ohm -> negligible ESR ripple. [KEY INSIGHT] Two-stage filtering: electrolytic handles bulk energy storage, ceramic handles HF ripple. Series L + parallel ceramic = most effective HF ripple killer. ESR of capacitor is often the limiting factor -- check datasheet.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The post-filter inductor and ceramic capacitor form a resonant trap specifically tuned to the switching frequency. The inductor acts as a high-frequency speed bump: it resists rapid changes in current (the ripple current). The ceramic capacitor then cleans up whatever ripple current makes it through by providing a very low-impedance (low ESR) path to GND for the remaining high-frequency energy. The key insight is that at 400kHz, an electrolytic capacitor's series resistance (0.1-1ohm) creates its own ripple voltage just from the ripple current flowing through it, making more electrolytics ineffective.

🔬 Physics

Electrolytic capacitor ripple voltage has two components: V_ripple = V_C + V_ESR. The capacitive component V_C = I_ripple/(2πfC) and the ESR component V_ESR = I_ripple × ESR. At 400kHz, for I_ripple = 0.5A and ESR = 0.1ohm: V_ESR = 50mV, which is already the full ripple budget! Adding more electrolytics reduces C-based ripple but ESR-based ripple can only be reduced by using lower-ESR components. Ceramic capacitors have ESR < 0.01ohm at 400kHz, making them 10-100× better for HF ripple filtering.

📐 Math

Post-filter corner: \( f_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{4.7\mu \times 10\mu}} = 733\,\text{Hz} \)

Attenuation at 400kHz: \( A = \left(\frac{f_0}{f_{sw}}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{733}{400k}\right)^2 = 3.4 \times 10^{-6} \) (practically -50dB)

ESR voltage: \( V_{ESR} = I_{ripple} \times ESR_{cap} \)

At 400kHz: electrolytic ESR ≈ 0.1Ω -> 50mV; ceramic ESR ≈ 5mΩ -> 2.5mV

🔧 Practical

Post-filter inductor: Murata LQH31CN4R7M04L (4.7uH, 1.1A, 0805). Ceramic capacitor: Samsung CL21B106KOQUNE (10uF X5R 0805, ESR < 5mΩ at 400kHz). Place post-filter as close as possible to the load. Verify with oscilloscope: use a 1:10 probe on the output, measure AC ripple in 10mV/div range with 400kHz bandwidth. DC blocking: use 100pF DC-blocking cap in series with oscilloscope probe to reject the 5V DC and only see the ripple.

📖 Story

In 2021, a Qualcomm reference design for Snapdragon 888 power delivery included a post-filter stage on the core voltage rail (0.9V, 30A, 2MHz switching). The single-stage output had 8mV ripple; the spec for the application processor was 3mV maximum. A single 1uH inductor and 220uF ceramic capacitor (four 47uF in parallel) added as a post-filter reduced ripple to 1.2mV, providing 9.5dB of headroom beyond the specification.

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope on buck output with 10mV/div, 400kHz bandwidth limit. Measure ripple amplitude. Step 2: Probe the capacitor ESR: measure voltage across the output cap directly (probe tip on + terminal, probe ground on GND directly at cap). 🚀 Optimize

For the most demanding applications (DAC/ADC references, VCO power supplies), use a fully linear regulator (LDO) as a post-filter instead of an LC network. An LDO with 1mV output ripple rejection (PSRR = 60dB at 400kHz) after a switching buck regulator gives total ripple below 50uV. The LDO adds 0.5-2V dropout voltage but provides much better transient response and noise performance than a passive LC filter.

Lec 12 — Scenario 20: LCD Ghost Image (Pixel Capacitor Discharge)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 20: LCD Ghost Image (Pixel Capacitor Discharge)

SCENARIO 20: "LCD display shows faint ghost image of previous screen content" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] TFT LCD driven by microcontroller. When screen changes from bright image to dark screen: Faint ghost of previous image visible for 2-5 seconds. Slowly fades away. [DIAGNOSIS] Q1: Ghost image = residual charge on LCD pixels. Q2: TIME problem. Very slow discharge (seconds). Q3: VOLTAGE problem. Residual voltage on pixel capacitors. Q4: LCD pixels = tiny capacitors. When display cleared: pixel caps must DISCHARGE through the pixel transistor + common electrode resistance. If discharge path R is high: tau = R * C_pixel is long. [SOLUTION] APPROACH 1 -- Reduce discharge tau: -> Ensure VCOM driver has low output impedance. -> Low R_th * C_pixel = short tau -> fast discharge. APPROACH 2 -- Active discharge circuit: VCOM --[R=1k]---- GND (switched via MOSFET during "clear screen" only) -> During clearing: MCU switches R -> forces fast discharge. -> After clearing: disconnect R. APPROACH 3 -- AC driving (standard LCD technique): -> Alternate pixel voltage polarity every frame. -> Average DC on pixels = 0 -> no image sticking. -> Built into proper LCD controllers (frame inversion). PASSIVE: VCOM --[C=100uF]-- Midpoint reference | [R=10k] | GND -> C holds VCOM stable at midpoint. -> R allows slow charge equalization. [KEY INSIGHT] LCD pixels are capacitors. Ghost images = capacitors not discharged. tau_discharge must be << frame time. Active discharge (switched R) solves what passive RC cannot. Always ask: is this HARDWARE (RC time constant) or SOFTWARE?

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

Each LCD pixel is a tiny capacitor charged to a voltage proportional to its brightness. When you send a "clear screen" command, you are asking all pixel capacitors to discharge to the neutral VCOM voltage. If the discharge path has high resistance (the common electrode driver has high output impedance, or the TFT transistor has high on-resistance), the RC time constant of the discharge is long — potentially seconds. The human eye can see residual brightness down to about 1% of full luminance, so the ghost is visible until the capacitors discharge to below 1% of their initial charge: t_ghost ≈ 5 × tau.

🔬 Physics

LCD pixel capacitance C_pixel = 1-10pF. VCOM common electrode has output impedance R_VCOM. The discharge time constant tau = R_VCOM × C_pixel. For R_VCOM = 10kohm (a resistor divider instead of proper op-amp buffer): tau = 10k × 10pF = 100ns. Seems fast, but 1 million pixels discharge simultaneously through the shared VCOM driver, creating a current I_total = N × C_pixel × (V_pixel/t), which causes voltage sag on VCOM if the driver cannot supply sufficient current. The voltage sag extends the effective discharge time.

📐 Math

Pixel discharge: \( V(t) = V_0 e^{-t/(R_{VCOM} C_{pixel})} \)

Ghost visible until: \( V(t_{clear}) < 0.01 V_0 \Rightarrow t_{clear} = 5\tau \)

Active discharge R: \( R_{discharge} = 1\text{k}\Omega \), tau = 1k × 10p = 10ns (per pixel)

Frame time (60fps): \( T_{frame} = 16.7\,\text{ms} \). Condition: \( 5\tau \ll T_{frame} \)

AC driving period: alternates polarity every frame → net DC = 0 → no charge accumulation

🔧 Practical

VCOM driver: use dedicated VCOM buffer IC (e.g., MAX5951) or configure a unity-gain op-amp buffer (OPA277) with output impedance <1ohm. Active discharge circuit: IRF9540N P-MOSFET + 1kohm resistor, controlled by MCU during display clear sequence (enable for 100us). AC driving: enable "frame inversion" in LCD controller register (write to VCOM inversion period register). Most modern LCD controllers (ILI9341, ST7789) have this built in.

📖 Story

In 2020, a Japanese consumer electronics manufacturer (Sharp, Osaka) investigated persistent ghost image complaints on their Aquos 8K television. The root cause was a VCOM driver design using a resistor divider (R_VCOM = 47kohm) instead of a low-impedance amplifier. At 8K resolution (7680×4320 = 33 million pixels), the total discharge current demand was 33M × 10pF × 5V / 1ms = 1.65A, far beyond the 0.07mA supply capacity of the resistor divider. Replacing the VCOM driver with a 2A op-amp buffer reduced ghost visibility time from 3 seconds to below 16ms (invisible to the eye).

🔍 Debug

Step 1: Oscilloscope on VCOM pin during display clear command. Measure VCOM voltage: if it sags during clearing, VCOM driver is overloaded. Step 2: Measure ghost visibility time with a stopwatch after displaying all-white then clearing to all-black. Human threshold is ~0.1% luminance. Step 3: Add 100uF capacitor at VCOM pin. If ghost time decreases, the VCOM driver has insufficient current. Step 4: Verify frame inversion is enabled: set LCD controller register and observe VCOM with oscilloscope. Should show alternating polarity every 16.7ms.

🚀 Optimize

The ultimate solution for zero image sticking in industrial or medical displays is to use IPS (In-Plane Switching) LCD technology, which has symmetric LC alignment and inherently lower DC offset sensitivity. IPS panels used in medical imaging (MRI workstations) combine AC driving with active VCOM buffering and achieve ghost image times below 1 frame period, enabling continuous 60fps display updates without any residual image.

Thevenin model: V_th (set voltage) + R_th (source impedance). Cheap supply = high R_th -> more voltage drop per mA.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

A cheap USB adapter has high Thevenin output impedance (R_th = 1-5 ohm) because its voltage regulation is poor. When your device draws 200mA in bursts, voltage sags by V = I × R_th = 200mA × 2ohm = 400mV, dropping from 5V to 4.6V — below USB spec. A laptop port has R_th <0.1ohm, so the same 200mA burst causes only 20mV sag. The bulk capacitor at the device input acts as a local energy buffer: it pre-charges to 5V from the slow adapter, then supplies the burst current locally without the adapter having to respond instantly.

🔬 Physics

The cheap adapter's output is V_adapter(t) = V_th - I_device(t) × R_th. During a 200mA current burst of 1ms duration, the adapter voltage sags and the capacitor supplies the deficit: I_cap = C × dV/dt. With C = 100uF: dV = I_burst × dt / C = 200mA × 1ms / 100uF = 2V sag — still too much! But the adapter partially compensates, reducing actual sag. The ferrite bead prevents switching ripple from the adapter's internal converter from reaching the device's sensitive circuits.

📐 Math

USB supply drop: \( \Delta V = I_{burst} \times R_{th} \)

Capacitor sag: \( \Delta V = \frac{I_{burst} \times t_{burst}}{C} \)

USB spec: V_VBUS \in [4.75V, 5.25V]

Required C: \( C = \frac{I_{burst} \times t_{burst}}{\Delta V_{max}} \)

Lec 12 — Scenario 22: BLDC Motor Kills WiFi (EMI Coexistence)

📄 Preserved from source — Lec 12 — Scenario 22: BLDC Motor Kills WiFi (EMI Coexistence)

SCENARIO 22: "Brushless motor controller causes WiFi module to disconnect" [WHAT YOU OBSERVE] Robot with BLDC motor + ESP32 WiFi, shared battery. WiFi drops connection every 30-60 seconds when motor runs. Motor controller uses 3-phase PWM at 20kHz. [DIAGNOSIS] PWM edges (fast) generate harmonics up into GHz range. Some harmonics fall in 2.4GHz WiFi band. Conducted noise on shared battery rail. Radiated EMI from motor wires. [SOLUTION] FRONT 1 -- Power isolation: Battery --[L=100uH]--+-- Motor controller +--[L=10uH]-- WiFi module (with 100uF+100nF) FRONT 2 -- Pi filter on each motor phase wire: Controller --[C=10nF GND]--[L=1uH]--[C=10nF GND]-- Motor FRONT 3 -- Physical layout: -> Twist motor wires (cancels magnetic field). -> Route motor wires away from WiFi antenna. -> Ground plane under WiFi module. [KEY INSIGHT] High-power switching circuits and sensitive RF must be treated as completely separate systems sharing only FILTERED supply. Fix EMI at the SOURCE first, then at the victim if needed.

Circuit Diagram

💡 Intuition

The motor controller's fast PWM edges are rich in harmonics: a 20kHz PWM with 100ns edges contains significant energy at 100kHz, 1MHz, 100MHz, and mathematically extends to 2.4GHz. The WiFi radio, sharing the same battery, receives these harmonics both through conducted paths (power supply) and radiated paths (motor wire acting as antenna). The solution is defense-in-depth: separate the power paths with inductors (so motor noise cannot travel backward to WiFi supply), then filter the motor wires at the source with a pi filter before they can radiate.

🔬 Physics

PWM edge spectrum: the Fourier series of a square wave with duty cycle D and period T has a sinc envelope. Above frequency f = 1/(pi*t_rise) (where t_rise is edge rise time), the spectral density falls as -40dB/decade. For t_rise = 100ns: spectral content extends to 3.2GHz. Twisting the motor wires reduces magnetic field because the equal and opposite currents in adjacent wires create dipole fields that cancel at distances greater than the twist pitch.

📐 Math

Edge bandwidth: \( f_{bw} = \frac{1}{\pi t_{rise}} \)

For \( t_{rise} = 100\text{ns} \): \( f_{bw} = 3.2\,\text{GHz} \) (reaches WiFi 2.4GHz band!)

Twisted pair rejection: \( \text{CMRR} \approx 20\log(d_{twist}/\lambda) \)

🔧 Practical

Motor pi filter: 10nF X7R 0805 to GND on each phase + 1uH drum core inductor + 10nF X7R on motor side. WiFi supply: 10uH shielded inductor (Coilcraft DO3316P-103MLD) + 100uF 10V X5R 1210 + 100nF X7R 0402. Twist motor wires: 6 twists per meter minimum.

📖 Story

In 2021, a Boston Dynamics-inspired quadruped robot startup (Ghost Robotics, Philadelphia) found WiFi dropping during fast gaits. 🔍 Debug

Step 1: WiFi RSSI (received signal strength) logging during motor operation. Note correlation. Step 2: Spectrum analyzer 0-3GHz with motor running: identify harmonic at 2.4GHz. 💡 Intuition

The MOSFET Miller capacitance is a capacitor connected between drain and gate. When the drain voltage slams down rapidly (dV/dt), charge flows through this capacitor directly into the gate — the MOSFET is trying to turn itself on by the rate of voltage change at its drain. This is particularly dangerous in half-bridge circuits where the complementary MOSFET is switching, creating exactly the fast dV/dt events that capacitively couple into the OFF transistor's gate. The gate-source bleeder resistor provides a discharge path for this injected charge before it can build up sufficient voltage to cross the threshold.
