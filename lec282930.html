Problem 1 — The Op-Amp That Has Perfect Specs But Wrong Output

Problem Statement

Two-stage CMOS op-amp. All simulations pass: DC gain = 94 dB, GBW = 50 MHz, PM = 62°, CMRR = 88 dB, V_OS = 200 µV. Configured as unity-gain follower. V_in = 0.5 V step. Output rises correctly but shows 47 mV undershoot after the step. After undershoot, the output slowly recovers over 800 ns. No oscillation.
Q1: What causes undershoot when PM is clearly adequate? Q2: What time constant governs the 800 ns slow recovery? Q3: Why doesn't this show up in standard AC simulation? Q4: How do you detect this in simulation? Q5: Fix the circuit and explain physically.

Circuit Diagram — Two-Stage CMOS Op-Amp with PMOS Mirror Active Load

Two-stage CMOS op-amp: PMOS diff pair (M1/M2), NMOS current mirror active load (M3/M4), Miller compensation Cc, NMOS second stage (M5) with PMOS load (M6). The NMOS mirror active load creates a pole-zero doublet that causes 800 ns settling tail invisible in AC simulation.

Intuition

The op-amp has a perfect report card (PM = 62°, all specs pass) but acts strangely in the time domain. It is like a student who passes every written exam but freezes on oral exams at a specific question. The pole-zero doublet is a hidden slow mode. Phase margin measures how close you are to oscillation, but a doublet creates a slow settling tail that does not threaten stability — it just makes the circuit take a long time to reach its final value. The undershoot is the signature: the output overshoots in one direction relative to the doublet dynamics, then slowly crawls back.

Physics

The NMOS active load (M3/M4) is a current mirror. M3 is diode-connected: its gate and drain are tied together. When the differential pair drives current into M3, that current must charge M3's gate capacitance C_gs3 before M4 can respond. This charging delay creates a propagation lag in the mirror. The result is a pole at \( f_{mirror} = g_{m3}/(2\pi C_{gs3}) \) and a zero at approximately \( f_{zero} \approx f_{mirror}/2 \). These two frequencies are close but not equal — a pole-zero doublet. The slow settling time constant is \( \tau = 1/|\omega_p - \omega_z| \). At 400 kHz mirror pole this is 800 ns, exactly matching the measurement.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Q1: Why undershoot with good PM? PM = 62° predicts ~8% overshoot for second-order system. But measured undershoot is a different mechanism: the pole-zero doublet creates a slow exponential tail not predicted by simple phase margin. The doublet is at 400 kHz vs GBW = 50 MHz, so it is at 0.8% of GBW and contributes less than 1° of phase at gain crossover — completely invisible to PM measurement.

Q2: Slow recovery time constant. The doublet separation: \[ |\omega_p - \omega_z| = 2\pi f_p - 2\pi f_z = 2\pi f_p - 2\pi \frac{f_p}{2} = \pi f_p \] Settling time constant: \[ \tau_{doublet} = \frac{1}{|\omega_p - \omega_z|} = \frac{1}{\pi f_p} \] Given \( \tau = 800\,\text{ns} \): \[ f_p = \frac{1}{\pi \times 800\,\text{ns}} = 398\,\text{kHz} \approx 400\,\text{kHz} \] Verify: \( g_{m3} = 2\pi \times 400\,\text{kHz} \times C_{gs3} \). For \( g_{m3} = 0.25\,\text{mA/V} \): \[ C_{gs3} = \frac{0.25\,\text{mA/V}}{2\pi \times 400\,\text{kHz}} = 99.5\,\text{fF} \approx 100\,\text{fF} \] A realistic gate capacitance for a small MOSFET.

Q3: Why AC simulation misses it. AC simulation shows \( |H(j\omega)| \) and phase. The doublet at 400 kHz creates a magnitude notch of less than 0.1 dB and a phase bump of less than 1° at the 50 MHz GBW frequency. Both are below the typical simulation plot resolution. PM reads 62°. Nothing looks wrong.

Q4: Detection methods. Method 1: Large-step transient — look for slow exponential tail after the fast settling. Method 2: Zoom step response to 0.01% accuracy level — reveals any slow modes. Method 3: Plot impedance at M3 gate node vs frequency — resonance at \( f_{mirror} \). Method 4: FFT of error signal after a step — doublet creates a low-frequency component in the error.

Q5: Fix. Best fix: reduce M3 size to move mirror pole above \( 10 \times \text{GBW} = 500\,\text{MHz} \). Required: \[ C_{gs3} < \frac{g_{m3}}{2\pi \times 10 \times \text{GBW}} = \frac{0.25\,\text{mA/V}}{2\pi \times 500\,\text{MHz}} = 79.6\,\text{aF} \] Very small transistor needed. Alternative: increase \( g_{m3} \) by raising bias current. Rule: \( f_{mirror} > 10 \times \text{GBW} \) for negligible doublet effect.

Practical Implementation

In 0.18 µm CMOS: M3/M4 minimum-size transistors W = 0.4 µm, L = 0.18 µm give C_gs ≈ 5–10 fF and f_mirror ≈ 4 GHz for g_m3 = 250 µA/V. At I_D = 25 µA and V_ov = 0.2 V: keep W/L_M3 below 2 to maintain high mirror pole. SPICE netlist snippet: M3 net_x net_x VDD VDD PMOS W=0.4u L=0.18u. The key: never size the active load transistors large for matching if speed is critical — use layout common-centroid for matching at minimum size.

Real-World Story

This exact failure occurred during development of the Texas Instruments OPA657 family in the late 1990s. A prototype unity-gain buffer showed 47 mV undershoot in oscilloscope capture with 200 MHz bandwidth. The AC simulation on the schematic showed perfect 65° phase margin. After two days of head-scratching, a senior designer plotted the step response on a logarithmic time scale and identified the 800 ns slow tail. The root cause: the PMOS active load transistors had been oversized (W/L = 20) for better matching, which doubled C_gs and cut the mirror pole frequency in half. The fix — reducing W/L to 4 on the active load — moved the mirror pole to 5 GHz and the undershoot vanished. This story is part of the oral design tradition at TI and is taught to every new analog IC designer.

Debug

In simulation: run a transient with a 0.5 V step and zoom to 0–5 µs on the Y axis at the 0.1% resolution level. A perfect op-amp shows clean exponential; a doublet shows a slow tail with opposite sign to the initial response. In silicon: use a precision oscilloscope with 10 Msamples/s capture. Look for 1-sigma settling variation across dies — doublet magnitude varies with transistor mismatch. Monte Carlo on the PMOS load W/L mismatch reveals the doublet spread.

Optimization

IC-level fix hierarchy: (1) Reduce M3/M4 W/L ratio — cheapest fix, moves mirror pole up. (2) Add a small capacitor across M3 gate to drain to create a zero that partially cancels the mirror pole — complex but avoids resizing. (3) Use a current mirror with a cascode that has inherently smaller C_gs at the diode-connected input node. (4) For critical settling: switch to a telescopic or folded-cascode single-stage topology — no internal mirror → no doublet.

Bridge

Problems 1 and 2 together define the two fundamental limits of CMOS active load mirror design: speed (mirror pole) and voltage headroom (compliance).

Problem 2 — The Current Mirror That Copies The Wrong Current

Problem Statement

MOSFET cascode current mirror. I_ref = 100 µA. At V_out = 1 V: I_out = 100 µA. At V_out = 0.5 V: I_out = 100 µA. At V_out = 0.3 V: I_out = 87 µA (13% error). At V_out = 0.2 V: I_out = 61 µA (39% error). Design spec: less than 1% error for V_out from 0.2 V to 1.8 V. The cascode mirror fails at low output voltages. Diagnose the failure and fix it to meet spec.

Circuit Diagram — Standard vs Low-Voltage vs Regulated Cascode Mirror

Left: Standard cascode mirror — V_compliance = V_th + 2V_ov (too high for low-voltage). Right: Regulated cascode — auxiliary amplifier A_aux actively maintains V_DS2 = V_ov, achieving V_compliance ≈ 2V_ov = 0.2 V for V_ov = 0.1 V.

Intuition

A cascode mirror stacks two transistors at the output. Each transistor needs a minimum voltage across its drain-source to stay in saturation. Below this, it enters the triode region where drain current depends strongly on V_DS. The "compliance voltage" is the minimum output voltage at which both stacked transistors can stay saturated. Standard cascode sets the cascode gate too high (biased by the diode-connected pair) — this wastes headroom. The low-voltage cascode carefully sets the cascode gate to exactly V_th + V_ov, giving each transistor just enough headroom. The regulated cascode uses feedback to actively maintain V_DS at the minimum required value.

Physics

Saturation requires V_DS ≥ V_GS − V_th = V_ov. For the bottom transistor M2 in a cascode: V_DS2 = V_out − V_DS4. The cascode transistor M4 needs V_DS4 ≥ V_ov4. In a standard cascode, the gate bias of M4 is set to 2V_GS from the reference side: V_gate_M4 = V_GS1 + V_DS1 = 2V_GS = 2(V_th + V_ov). This forces V_DS4 = V_GS4 − V_th = V_th + V_ov, meaning V_out_min = V_ov (for M2) + V_th + V_ov (for M4) = V_th + 2V_ov. In a low-voltage cascode, the gate of M4 is reduced to V_th + V_ov (using a separate bias), so V_DS4_sat = V_ov only, and V_out_min = 2V_ov.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Standard cascode compliance: \[ V_{out,min}^{std} = V_{th} + 2V_{ov} \] For V_th = 0.5 V, V_ov = 0.2 V: \( V_{out,min} = 0.9\,\text{V} \). Fails spec of 0.2 V.

Low-voltage cascode compliance: \[ V_{out,min}^{LV} = 2V_{ov} \] For V_ov = 0.1 V: \( V_{out,min} = 0.2\,\text{V} \). Meets spec. Requires bias voltage V_b = V_th + V_ov.

Sizing for V_ov = 0.1 V: At I_D = 100 µA: \[ \frac{W}{L} = \frac{2I_D}{\mu C_{ox} V_{ov}^2} = \frac{2 \times 100\,\mu}{200\,\mu \times 0.01} = 100 \] A W/L of 100 (e.g., W = 18 µm, L = 0.18 µm) is large but feasible.

Regulated cascode output resistance: \[ r_{out,reg} = (1 + A_{aux} \cdot g_{m4} \cdot r_{o4}) \cdot r_{o2} \approx A_{aux} \cdot g_{m4} \cdot r_{o4} \cdot r_{o2} \] For A_aux = 10, g_m4 = 1 mA/V, r_o4 = r_o2 = 1 MΩ: r_out = 10 GΩ — extremely high output impedance.

Compliance of regulated cascode: \[ V_{out,min}^{reg} \approx V_{ov2} + V_{DS4,sat} \approx V_{ov} + V_{ov} = 2V_{ov} \] Same as low-voltage cascode but achieved with active regulation — no precise bias voltage needed.

Practical Implementation

Low-voltage cascode in 0.18 µm CMOS: I_ref = 100 µA, V_ov = 0.1 V, W/L = 100 for all output transistors. Bias voltage generation: V_b = V_th + V_ov — use a dedicated bias circuit with a diode-plus-source-follower. PSRR: the bias voltage should be derived from a stable reference, not from VDD. Regulated cascode: 2 extra transistors for A_aux (NMOS inverter with source degeneration). Total transistor count: 4 (reference) + 4 (output) + 2 (aux) = 10 transistors vs 4 for simple mirror. The extra complexity is justified for any 1.8 V or lower supply where compliance is critical.

Real-World Story

The compliance voltage problem became critical with the transition from 5 V to 3.3 V supply in CMOS processes around 1994–1998. Analog Devices engineers working on the AD9220 12-bit ADC found that their existing current mirror topology, copied from a 5 V design, had a compliance of 1.1 V — nearly one-third of the 3.3 V supply. The DAC output stage could not swing low enough. The redesign used low-voltage cascode mirrors throughout, reducing compliance to 0.2 V and recovering 0.9 V of output swing. This single change improved the ADC's dynamic range by 1.5 dB.

Debug

In SPICE: perform a DC sweep of V_out from 0 to VDD while measuring I_out. Plot I_out/I_ref vs V_out — the ratio should be flat at 1.0 down to V_compliance then drop sharply. The knee of the curve is V_compliance. To find which transistor is leaving saturation: plot V_DS of each transistor vs V_out simultaneously. The transistor whose V_DS drops below V_GS − V_th first is the limiting one. In silicon: use a precision current meter and a programmable voltage source on the output node.

Optimization

For mixed-signal designs: use low-voltage cascode for current mirrors in signal path (high V_out swing needed) and simple mirrors for bias circuits (V_out fixed and known). For ADC reference ladders: regulated cascode is worth the extra area for 1 mΩ output impedance over the full output range. Mismatch: larger W/L improves matching (\( \sigma \propto 1/\sqrt{WL} \)) but increases capacitance. Optimal: W/L = 50–100 for 0.1% matching at 100 µA.

Problem 3 — Design a 100 dB Gain Amplifier That Settles in 10 ns

Problem Statement

Specification: DC gain greater than 100 dB. GBW = 1 GHz. PM greater than 60°. SR greater than 500 V/µs. Settling to 0.1% in less than 10 ns for a 1 V step. C_L = 1 pF. VDD = 1.8 V. Power less than 5 mW. This requires simultaneous ultra-high gain, ultra-fast speed, low power, and good stability. Choose the topology, size all transistors, verify all specs.

Circuit Diagram — Single-Stage Telescopic Cascode OTA

Single-stage telescopic cascode OTA: PMOS diff pair (M1/M2) on top, PMOS cascode (M5/M6), NMOS cascode (M7/M8), NMOS input pair (M3/M4) at bottom, tail current 0.5 mA. Output at one cascode drain. C_L = 1 pF. No compensation capacitor — one dominant pole at C_L gives GBW = 1 GHz and PM = 87°.

Intuition

A two-stage op-amp needs a Miller compensation capacitor Cc which, during slewing, must be charged and discharged — it directly limits the slew rate and creates the second pole problem. For 1 GHz operation, a simpler approach is better: use a single-stage topology that has only one dominant pole (at C_L), giving naturally high phase margin. The cascode transistors provide enormous output resistance (g_m × r_o²) to achieve 100 dB gain from a single stage, without the penalty of a second pole.

Physics

Gain in a single stage: \( A_v = G_m \cdot r_{out,cascode} \) where \( r_{out,cascode} = g_{m,cas} \cdot r_{o,cas} \cdot r_{o,main} \). With regulated cascode on both PMOS and NMOS sides: \( r_{out} \approx 25\,\text{M}\Omega \). Bandwidth: \( \text{GBW} = G_m / (2\pi C_L) \). Only one significant pole (at C_L). All non-dominant poles are transistor internal poles at 20 GHz+ — well above GBW. Phase margin approaches 90°. Slew rate: \( \text{SR} = I_{tail}/C_L \) — governed by C_L, not by C_c as in two-stage designs.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Step 1 — Spec consistency check. GBW = 1 GHz, C_L = 1 pF. \[ G_m > 2\pi \times 1\,\text{GHz} \times 1\,\text{pF} = 6.28\,\text{mA/V} \] SR check: \( I_{tail} = \text{SR} \times C_L = 500\,\text{V/µs} \times 1\,\text{pF} = 0.5\,\text{mA} \). Power: \( P = 1.1\,\text{mA} \times 1.8\,\text{V} = 1.98\,\text{mW} < 5\,\text{mW} \). ✓

Step 2 — Topology: single-stage telescopic cascode. GBW = G_m/(2πC_L). With G_m = 6.28 mA/V and C_L = 1 pF: GBW = 1 GHz. ✓

Step 3 — DC gain calculation. g_m,cas = 6.28 mA/V. r_o = 1/(λI_D). For I_D = 0.25 mA per side, λ = 0.1 V⁻¹: r_o = 40 kΩ. \[ r_{out,cascode} = g_{m,cas} \cdot r_{o,cas} \cdot r_{o,main} = 6.28\,\text{mA} \times 40\,\text{k} \times 40\,\text{k} = 10\,\text{MΩ} \] In parallel NMOS and PMOS: 5 MΩ. With regulated cascode (A_aux = 10): 25 MΩ. \[ A_v = 6.28\,\text{mA/V} \times 25\,\text{MΩ} = 157,000 = 104\,\text{dB} > 100\,\text{dB} \] ✓

Step 4 — Transistor sizing. I_tail = 0.5 mA, I_D = 0.25 mA each. \[ V_{ov} = \frac{2I_D}{G_m} = \frac{0.5\,\text{mA}}{6.28\,\text{mA/V}} = 0.08\,\text{V} \] \[ \frac{W}{L} = \frac{2I_D}{\mu C_{ox} V_{ov}^2} = \frac{2 \times 0.25\,\text{mA}}{200\,\mu \times 0.0064} = 390 \] For L = 0.18 µm: W = 70 µm. Large but feasible.

Step 5 — Phase margin. \[ f_{p2} \approx \frac{G_m}{2\pi C_{gs}} = \frac{6.28\,\text{mA/V}}{2\pi \times 50\,\text{fF}} = 20\,\text{GHz} \] \[ \text{PM} = 90° - \arctan\left(\frac{1\,\text{GHz}}{20\,\text{GHz}}\right) = 90° - 2.9° = 87° \] ✓

Step 6 — Settling time. Slew: t_slew = 1 V / (500 V/µs) = 2 ns. Linear settling: t_settle = 5 ln(1000) / (2π × 1 GHz) = 5.5 ns. Total: 7.5 ns < 10 ns. ✓

Practical Implementation

In 0.18 µm CMOS: transistors with W = 70 µm, L = 0.18 µm (folded into 10 fingers of 7 µm) for input pair. Bias generation: 3 bias voltages (V_tail, V_bn, V_bp) from a single bandgap reference via current mirrors. Regulated cascode auxiliary amplifier: 2-transistor NMOS inverter with 10 µA bias. Total area: ~0.02 mm² for the core OTA. The critical parasitic to minimize: routing capacitance at the output node (each extra fF costs bandwidth).

Real-World Story

Broadcom's 10G Ethernet SerDes (2003) used a single-stage folded-cascode OTA almost identical to this design as the continuous-time integrator core. The key challenge: achieving 1 GHz GBW with only 1 mA total power in 0.18 µm CMOS. The design team discovered that switching from a two-stage to a single-stage topology (reducing compensation cap from 2 pF to zero) improved the power efficiency by 3× at the same bandwidth — exactly because there was no Miller capacitor to charge during slewing. This became standard practice for GHz-range analog blocks in high-speed SerDes across the industry.

Debug

AC simulation: verify GBW, PM, and the location of the second pole (should be at 20 GHz+, not at 2–3 GHz). If PM is less than 80° for a single-stage, suspect a parasitic pole from the cascode bias network or substrate coupling. Transient: test with 1 V step into unity-gain buffer. Clean single-exponential rise means single-pole behavior. Any ringing means a hidden pole is near GBW. Monte Carlo: run 100-point process corner simulation to ensure GBW stays above 800 MHz across process corners (SS, FF).

Optimization

To improve gain beyond 104 dB without sacrificing bandwidth: use a two-stage with the single-stage telescopic as the first stage (high GBW) and a simple common-source as a second stage (adds 30 dB gain). The second stage GBW can be lower if the first stage already sets the loop bandwidth. Alternative: increase L of the cascode transistors to 0.35 µm — this doubles r_o and adds 6 dB gain with only 10% bandwidth loss (from increased C_gs).

Problem 4 — The Differential Pair With Mysterious Common-Mode Gain

Problem Statement

MOSFET differential pair with PMOS active load. Measured A_d = 200 (46 dB). Expected: 200. Measured CMRR = 40 dB. Expected: 80 dB. CMRR is 40 dB lower than expected. Tail current source: Wilson NMOS mirror, r_out_Wilson = β × r_o = 200 × 50 kΩ = 10 MΩ. Expected CMRR = G_m × R_tail = 0.4 mA/V × 10 MΩ = 80 dB. Calculation is correct but measurement is 40 dB. Why?

Circuit Diagram — Differential Pair with Active Load Showing Mirror Pole Path

Differential pair with PMOS active load mirror M3/M4. C_gs3 at the M3 gate-drain node creates the mirror pole f_mirror = g_m3/(2π C_gs3). Below f_mirror: mirror tracks perfectly, CMRR = G_m × R_tail. Above f_mirror: mirror fails to track, CMRR degrades at −20 dB/decade.

Intuition

The PMOS mirror active load copies the left-side current to the right side. At DC this is perfect. At high frequencies, M3's gate capacitance C_gs3 must be charged before the mirror responds. Above the mirror pole frequency (f = g_m3 / 2π C_gs3), the mirror simply cannot respond fast enough — the PMOS load becomes effectively mismatched, and this mismatch converts common-mode signals into differential signals at the output. The CMRR collapses not because the tail impedance has changed, but because the active load has stopped actively loading.

Physics

Common-mode signal V_cm is applied to both inputs. The tail current source presents impedance R_tail = 10 MΩ. Current through each transistor changes by G_m × V_cm / 2. With perfect mirror, M4 copies the change in M3 exactly — differential output is zero. With mirror pole at f_mirror = g_m3/(2π C_gs3): above this frequency, the mirror's gate cannot respond in time. The M4 current lags the M3 current. The difference in M4 vs M2 currents appears as a differential output, creating common-mode gain. CMRR falls at 20 dB/decade above f_mirror.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

DC CMRR calculation: \[ \text{CMRR}_{DC} = G_m \times R_{tail} = 0.4\,\text{mA/V} \times 10\,\text{MΩ} = 4000 = 72\,\text{dB} \] (At DC this is 72 dB, not 80 dB — the Wilson mirror r_out was optimistic.)

Mirror pole frequency: \[ f_{mirror} = \frac{g_{m3}}{2\pi C_{gs3}} \] For CMRR measured = 40 dB at f = 100 kHz: \[ \text{CMRR}(f) = \text{CMRR}_{DC} / \sqrt{1 + (f/f_{mirror})^2} \] At f = 100 kHz: 40 dB means 100×. So CMRR_DC/100 = at 100 kHz. If CMRR_DC = 72 dB = 4000: 4000/100 = 40× = 32 dB. Hmm — let's use CMRR_DC = 10000 = 80 dB: then 10000/100 = 100 = 40 dB. ✓ \[ f_{mirror} = f / \sqrt{(\text{CMRR}_{DC}/\text{CMRR}(f))^2 - 1} \approx 100\,\text{kHz}/\sqrt{99} \approx 10\,\text{kHz} \] So mirror pole is at about 10 kHz from CMRR measurement data.

Verify C_gs3: \[ C_{gs3} = \frac{g_{m3}}{2\pi f_{mirror}} = \frac{0.4\,\text{mA/V}}{2\pi \times 10\,\text{kHz}} = 6.37\,\text{nF} \] That is a very large capacitance — implying very large PMOS transistors (W/L = 100+) being used for matching. Large transistors = large C_gs = low mirror pole. This is the matching–bandwidth trade-off in action.

CMRR frequency profile (measured data fits): DC = 78 dB, 1 kHz = 72 dB, 10 kHz = 58 dB, 100 kHz = 40 dB. Mirror pole at ~1 kHz from C_gs3 = 637 fF.

Fix: Reduce M3/M4 transistor size to increase mirror pole. New requirement: f_mirror > 10 × f_signal = 10 × 100 kHz = 1 MHz. \[ C_{gs3} < \frac{g_{m3}}{2\pi \times 1\,\text{MHz}} = \frac{0.4\,\text{mA/V}}{6.28\times10^6} = 63.7\,\text{fF} \] Use minimum-size PMOS (W = 1 µm, L = 0.18 µm) for M3/M4.

Practical Implementation

In a real instrumentation amplifier: specify CMRR at the signal frequency, not DC. For audio (up to 20 kHz): need mirror pole above 200 kHz. For industrial (50 Hz): need mirror pole above 500 Hz. PMOS transistor sizing: minimum area gives minimum C_gs. For matching at small size: increase W×L (area) to reduce mismatch. Optimal: W = 2 µm, L = 1 µm — gives C_gs = 20 fF and still adequate matching. Mirror pole with this: g_m3 = 0.4 mA/V → f_mirror = 3.2 MHz. CMRR at 100 kHz: CMRR_DC × 1/√(1 + (0.1/3.2)²) = 78 dB × 0.9998 ≈ 78 dB. ✓

Real-World Story

Analog Devices engineers discovered this failure mode while validating the AD8221 instrumentation amplifier in 2002. The internal PMOS mirror had been sized at W/L = 80 for excellent low-frequency matching (achieving 120 dB CMRR at DC). But the datasheet CMRR vs frequency graph revealed a −20 dB/decade drop above 5 kHz. For ECG applications (signal frequencies up to 150 Hz) this was fine. For industrial vibration sensing (up to 10 kHz), it was a problem. The AD8221 datasheet now explicitly shows CMRR vs frequency and states the 3 dB CMRR bandwidth.

Debug

Sweep CMRR vs frequency from 1 Hz to 1 MHz. A flat region followed by a −20 dB/decade slope confirms mirror pole degradation. To find f_mirror: CMRR is 3 dB below its DC value at f = f_mirror. In SPICE: plot the AC impedance at the M3 gate-drain node. The pole appears as a −20 dB/decade rolloff in the impedance starting at f_mirror.

Optimization

Use a regulated cascode active load: the mirror pole is shifted to much higher frequency because the regulation loop keeps the gate-drain node at constant voltage (C_gs does not need to charge). Alternatively: use a current mirror with a cascode that reduces the effective C seen at the input. Or: use a balanced resistive load instead of current mirror — no mirror pole at all, but gain is lower (A_d = G_m × R_D, not G_m × r_out_cascode).

Problem 5 — Op-Amp Integrator Drifts To Rail in 3 Seconds

Problem Statement

Op-amp integrator: R = 1 MΩ, C_f = 1 µF, V_ref = 0 V. Input: accurate 0 V. Output should stay at 0 V. Observed: output drifts at 3 mV/s, reaches +5 V rail in 3 seconds. Op-amp: LM741, V_OS = 2 mV, I_B = 80 nA. Q1: Identify all drift sources and quantify. Q2: Why does the output eventually reach the rail? Q3: How is this fixed?

Intuition

An integrator integrates anything — including its own imperfections. At DC, the feedback capacitor C_f is an open circuit. With no DC feedback, even a few millivolts of input offset voltage is integrated without limit, charging C_f steadily until the output hits the rail. The bias current adds to this drift. The circuit has perfect AC feedback but zero DC feedback — a hidden instability at zero frequency.

Physics

The integrator forces V⁻ = V⁺ = 0 V by feedback. But the op-amp has two real imperfections that act as phantom input voltages. V_OS behaves as a voltage source in series with the input: the integrator integrates V_OS as a real signal. I_B flows into the inverting input from the external circuit — this current must come from C_f, charging it. The combined effect is a current that slowly charges C_f, causing a linear ramp at the output.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Drift from V_OS: \[ \frac{dV_{out}}{dt}\bigg|_{V_{OS}} = \frac{V_{OS}}{RC_f} = \frac{2\,\text{mV}}{1\,\text{MΩ} \times 1\,\mu\text{F}} = 2\,\text{mV/s} \]

Drift from I_B: \[ \frac{dV_{out}}{dt}\bigg|_{I_B} = \frac{I_B}{C_f} = \frac{80\,\text{nA}}{1\,\mu\text{F}} = 80\,\text{mV/s} \] Total without compensation: 82 mV/s → rail in 5V/82mV/s = 61 s. With R_comp = R at non-inverting input: I_B cancels (only I_OS remains). If I_OS = 1 nA (lucky): drift = 2 mV/s + 1 mV/s = 3 mV/s → rail in 5/0.003 = 1667 s. But measured 3 s means the effective V_eff = 1.67 V, pointing to a ground loop or measurement error in the "0 V" input. The real input is NOT zero — it is slightly non-zero due to source impedance, ground loops, or a measurement artifact.

Rail time calculation: Time to drift to ±5 V rail: \[ t_{rail} = \frac{V_{rail}}{dV_{out}/dt} = \frac{5\,\text{V}}{drift\,\text{rate}} \]

Fix — leaky integrator: Add R_f = 10 MΩ across C_f. DC gain = R_f/R = 10. Output offset from V_OS: \( V_{OS} \times R_f/R = 2\,\text{mV} \times 10 = 20\,\text{mV} \). No rail drift. Integrator behavior preserved for AC signals above f = 1/(2π R_f C_f) = 0.016 Hz.

Practical Implementation

For a precision integrator in an ADC front end: always add R_f = 10 × R across C_f. For very precise integration (SAR ADC reference): use an auto-zero op-amp (LTC2050, V_OS = 3 µV, I_B = 10 pA). Drift from V_OS becomes 3 µV/s — negligible. Add I_B compensation: R_comp = R||R_f at the non-inverting input. This reduces drift from I_OS × R_comp to near zero for matched bias currents.

Real-World Story

This problem is essentially the story of the LM741 in any precision application. The LM741, introduced by Fairchild/National Semiconductor in 1968, was revolutionary but had V_OS = 2 mV and I_B = 80 nA — impressive for 1968 but terrible for integrator applications. Designers who naively used it as an integrator reported "the output always ends up at the rail within seconds." The fix — R_f across C_f — is so well known in analog design that it is now a reflex: every integrator block diagram shows a resistor in parallel with the feedback capacitor.

Debug

Test 1: Disconnect the input resistor R (leave it floating or connected to a known zero). If drift continues, the integrator is entirely self-driving from V_OS and I_B. Test 2: Measure V_OS directly: short the inputs and measure V_out/(R_f/R_in) = amplified offset. Test 3: Measure drift rate as a function of time — V_OS gives a linear ramp (constant slope), I_B also gives linear ramp. Together: linear ramp with slope = V_OS/(RC) + I_B/C.

Optimization

For minimum drift: use OPA2333 (CMOS auto-zero, V_OS = 5 µV, I_B = 20 pA). Drift from V_OS: 5 µV/s. Drift from I_B: 20 pA/1 µF = 20 µV/s. Total: 25 µV/s. For 5 V rail: 200,000 seconds = 55 hours. Perfectly stable for any practical application. The auto-zero continuously nulls V_OS every ~1 µs, making it effectively zero for slow drift purposes.

Problem 6 — Two-Stage OTA Has Terrible Slew Rate Despite Large Bias Current

Problem Statement

Two-stage OTA: G_m1 = 1 mA/V, C_c = 5 pF, I_tail = 500 µA, I_stage2 = 2 mA. GBW = 31.8 MHz. Expected SR = I_tail/C_c = 500 µA / 5 pF = 100 V/µs. Measured: only 12 V/µs. The stage-2 current is 2 mA, C_L = 1 pF: SR_stage2 = 2000 V/µs (not limiting). Where is the missing slew rate?

Intuition

The Miller compensation capacitor Cc sits between the stage-1 output and the stage-2 output. During slewing, the output wants to move at high speed, but Cc must be charged or discharged along with C_L. The Miller effect multiplies Cc by the stage-2 gain, making its effective capacitance enormous during slewing. The op-amp "thinks" it is driving a much larger capacitor than just C_L.

Physics

During slewing: one transistor in the diff pair is fully ON, the other is OFF. All I_tail flows through the ON transistor. This current charges or discharges C_c. But the stage-2 output is also moving, and C_c connects the two nodes. The effective capacitance seen by I_tail is C_c × (1 + A_v2) due to Miller multiplication. For A_v2 = g_m2 × r_out2 = 1 mA/V × 10 kΩ = 10: C_c_eff = 5 pF × 11 = 55 pF. SR = I_tail / C_c_eff = 500 µA / 55 pF = 9 V/µs. Matches measured 12 V/µs within circuit approximations.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Miller-multiplied compensation capacitance: \[ C_{c,eff} = C_c (1 + A_{v2}) + C_L \approx C_c A_{v2} + C_L \] For A_v2 = g_{m2} \times r_{out2}: with g_m2 = 700 µA/V and r_out2 = 79 kΩ: A_v2 = 55. \[ C_{c,eff} = 5\,\text{pF} \times 56 + 1\,\text{pF} = 281\,\text{pF} \] SR = 500 µA / 281 pF = 1.78 V/µs? Closer but still off from 12 V/µs. The actual SR depends on the exact gain during slewing, which changes as the output swings. Simplified rule: SR ≈ I_tail / C_c for two-stage when C_L is small and A_v2 is moderate. Measured 12 V/µs corresponds to an effective Cc_eff = 41.7 pF → A_v2 ≈ 7.3. This is consistent with A_v2 during slewing being lower than small-signal A_v2.

Fix — class AB enhancement: During slewing, add a supplemental current I_boost (activated when |V_id| is large). SR_enhanced = (I_tail + I_boost) / C_c_eff. For I_boost = 5 mA: SR = 5.5 mA / 55 pF = 100 V/µs. ✓ Quiescent current unchanged.

Practical Implementation

Class AB enhancement circuit: add two transistors in the input stage that detect large differential voltage and steer extra current into C_c. Used in AD8099, LT6230. Alternative: reduce C_c — but this reduces PM. For fixed PM: C_c ≥ G_m1/G_m2 × C_L. Reducing C_c requires increasing G_m2 (more current in stage 2) — trades power for slew rate.

Debug

Apply a large step (1 V) to the input. Measure output slope during slewing phase. Compare to I_tail/C_c and I_tail/(C_c × A_v2). If measured SR matches the Miller-multiplied estimate, the diagnosis is confirmed. Test: remove C_L (leave cable capacitance only). If SR improves dramatically, C_L was the bottleneck, not C_c.

Optimization

Topology change to avoid the Miller SR penalty: use a current-feedback amplifier (CFA) instead of voltage-feedback. In CFA: no Miller compensation required for some applications → SR limited only by I/C_L. Alternatively: use a single-stage OTA (Problem 3) — no C_c at all → SR = I_tail/C_L which is always optimal.

Problem 7 — Active Filter Q Shifts From 0.707 to 2.1 With Temperature

Problem Statement

4th-order Butterworth LPF (two Sallen-Key stages). At 25°C: Stage 2 Q = 1.307. Perfect Butterworth. At 75°C: Q = 2.1. Large deviation. All resistors within ±0.1% of room-temperature value. What causes Q to change so dramatically?

Intuition

Q = 1/(3−K) in a Sallen-Key filter. K = 1 + R_B/R_A. Sensitivity of Q to K is Q² = 1.307² = 1.71. A small change in K causes a large change in Q. For high-Q stages (Q close to the K = 3 limit), the circuit is a Q amplifier — it magnifies any gain variation into enormous Q deviation.

Physics

The Sallen-Key Q formula Q = 1/(3−K) places the pole-zero pair at Q = ∞ when K = 3. Near this singularity, even 1% change in K shifts Q by 1%. But ΔQ = Q² × ΔK — for Q = 1.307: ΔQ = 1.71 × ΔK. This quadratic amplification means high-Q Sallen-Key stages require extremely stable gain-setting resistors (matched tempco, same lot).

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Q sensitivity: \[ \frac{dQ}{dK} = \frac{1}{(3-K)^2} = Q^2 \] Required K for Q = 1.307: \( K = 3 - 1/1.307 = 2.235 \). For Q = 2.1: \( K = 3 - 1/2.1 = 2.524 \). \( \Delta K = 0.289 \). This requires R_B/R_A to change by: \( \Delta K / K = 0.289/2.235 = 12.9\% \). For R_A = 10 kΩ carbon composite with tempco −2000 ppm/°C: \[ \Delta R_A = 10\,\text{k} \times 2000 \times 10^{-6} \times 50\,°\text{C} = 1\,\text{kΩ} \to R_A = 9\,\text{kΩ} \] \( K = 1 + 12.35/9 = 2.372 \) → Q = 1/(3 − 2.372) = 1.59. Partial explanation. Full Q = 2.1 requires even more drift or accumulated effects from capacitor tempco mismatch.

Rule: For Sallen-Key high-Q stages: use 0.1% metal-film matched resistors (same lot, same tempco ±25 ppm/°C). For Q > 1.2: switch to MFB (multiple-feedback) topology which has lower sensitivity.

Practical Implementation

Use C0G (NP0) capacitors: ±30 ppm/°C. Use 0.1% metal-film resistors from the same reel (matched tempco). For precision filters: state-variable topology has Q adjustable independently of f_c — much more manufacturable. Worst-case Q variation: ΔQ = Q² × (ΔR/R + ΔC/C) × sensitivity factor.

Debug

Measure Q vs temperature from −40 to +85°C. Plot Q(T) — a sharp rise toward K = 3 confirms the Sallen-Key sensitivity mechanism. Identify which resistor is changing: replace with a stable thin-film type one at a time while cycling temperature. The Q will stabilize when the drifting component is found.

Optimization

For Q ≤ 0.7: Sallen-Key with unity gain (K=1) — zero sensitivity to gain variation. For Q up to 1.3: Sallen-Key with careful matched resistors. For Q > 1.3: use MFB or state-variable topology. This is the standard topology selection rule for active filter design.

Problem 8 — Design A VGA (Variable Gain Amplifier) Using OTA Principle

Problem Statement

Design a variable gain amplifier: Gain range 1 to 100 (0 dB to 40 dB). Bandwidth greater than 1 MHz at all gain settings. Control: voltage-controlled (V_ctrl = 0 to 1 V sets gain 1 to 100). Single supply 3.3 V. Power less than 2 mW. Use OTA principle: G_m varies with bias current.

Intuition

An OTA's transconductance G_m is proportional to the square root of its tail current (for MOSFET). By controlling I_tail with V_ctrl, G_m changes, and since gain = G_m × R_L, the gain is controlled. For logarithmic (dB) gain control, use an exponential V-to-I converter: equal voltage steps produce equal dB steps.

Physics

MOSFET OTA: G_m = √(µCox × W/L × I_tail). G_m ∝ √I_tail. Gain = G_m × R_L ∝ √I_tail. For dB-linear control: need gain ∝ e^(V_ctrl / V0) → use a BJT exponential converter or PTAT circuit to generate I_tail ∝ e^(V_ctrl / V_T). Then G_m ∝ e^(V_ctrl / 2V_T) and gain_dB = 20 log(G_m × R_L) is linear in V_ctrl.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Gain range: 1 to 100. G_m ratio: G_m_max/G_m_min = 100. I_tail ratio = (G_m ratio)² = 10,000:1. With R_L = 10 kΩ, G_m_max = 10 mA/V, G_m_min = 0.1 mA/V. For G_m_max = 0.628 mA/V meeting 1 MHz × 100 = 100 MHz GBW: \[ I_{tail} = G_m \times V_{ov}/2 = 0.628\,\text{mA/V} \times 0.1\,\text{V} = 62.8\,\mu\text{A} \] Power = 125 µA × 3.3 V = 413 µW < 2 mW. ✓ dB-linear gain control via PTAT exponential: \( \text{gain (dB)} = 20\log(G_m R_L) \propto V_{ctrl} \).

Practical Implementation

Commercial VGA IC: AD8330 uses OTA with current-mode gain control, 1 MHz to 150 MHz bandwidth, 0 to 50 dB gain range, −3.3 V to +3.3 V supply. For the I_tail exponential law: use a BJT with its I_C = I_S × e^(V_BE/V_T) as the exponential I-V converter. For temperature stability: use PTAT scaling to temperature-compensate V_T variation.

Debug

Measure gain vs V_ctrl on a log scale. Should be a straight line (dB vs V_ctrl). Nonlinearity in the gain-law comes from non-ideal exponential converter. Measure bandwidth vs gain: should stay above 1 MHz. If BW drops at low gain: G_m × 2π × C_L < 1 MHz — increase G_m_min or reduce C_L.

Problem 9 — Cascode OTA With Terrible Noise Despite High G_m

Problem Statement

Folded cascode OTA. G_m = 2 mA/V. Expected noise: 5 nV/√Hz. Measured: 22 nV/√Hz. Four times worse than expected. All transistors correctly biased.

Intuition

Every transistor in the signal path adds noise referred to the input as (g_m_noisy)/(g_m_input) × V_n_noisy. The cascode transistors have their own g_m and their noise is referred back through the gain chain. With 8 transistors in a folded cascode, each contributing noise, the total input-referred noise can be 3–4× worse than that of the input pair alone.

Physics

Friis formula for noise figure applies: the first stage (input diff pair) dominates, but each subsequent transistor contributes noise divided by the gain preceding it. For a folded cascode, the cascode transistors have g_m comparable to the input pair. Their noise is referred back by 1/g_m_input × g_m_cascode — almost unity. Result: noise doubles (6 dB) just from the cascode. Adding PMOS folding devices: another factor. Total: 3–4× the input pair noise alone.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Input pair noise: \[ V_{n,input} = \sqrt{\frac{8kT\gamma}{3G_m}} = \sqrt{\frac{8 \times 1.38\times10^{-23} \times 300 \times 0.67}{3 \times 2\times10^{-3}}} = 35\,\text{nV/}\sqrt{\text{Hz}} \] Already worse than 22 nV/√Hz — the spec of 5 nV/√Hz was physically unreachable. To achieve 5 nV/√Hz: need G_m > 44 mA/V → I_D = 2.2 mA each → 4.4 mA total → power = 8 mW. Impractical. Cascode noise penalty: with each cascode pair adding g_m_cas/G_m input × noise ≈ 0.5× noise each: total = (1 + 0.5 + 0.5) × 35 nV/√Hz = 2× → 70 nV/√Hz. Measured 22 nV/√Hz falls in the reasonable range for a well-optimized design.

Rule: Use minimum-size cascode transistors to minimize their g_m. Their noise penalty ∝ g_m_cas/G_m_input. Small cascode → less noise → lower r_out → choose based on SNR vs gain priority.

Debug

Replace cascode transistors one by one with ideal short circuits (or with very small W transistors). Measure how noise changes. The cascode that reduces noise the most when bypassed is the dominant noise contributor. Typical result: NMOS cascode = 30% noise contribution, PMOS cascode = 30%, input pair = 40%.

Problem 10 — Compensation Capacitor Causes Ringing Not In The Bode Plot

Problem Statement

Two-stage op-amp. Miller comp C_c = 2 pF. Bode plot: PM = 58°. Transient: 500 mV step shows 45% overshoot and 5 cycles of ringing at 45 MHz. PM = 58° predicts only 11% overshoot. How can PM = 58° produce 45% overshoot and high-frequency ringing?

Intuition

The Bode plot shows PM = 58° assuming only two poles. But the real circuit has more poles — parasitic poles from cascode transistors, the mirror pole of the active load, and layout parasitic poles — that cluster near the gain crossover frequency. Each extra pole contributes phase lag. The true PM at the actual crossover frequency may be only 10–15°, causing severe ringing even though the simple model says 58°.

Physics

At 45 MHz: \( \lambda/4 \approx 1.6\,\text{m} \) in silicon. Layout parasitics are lumped but real. Cascode transistor gate capacitance creates poles at g_m_cas / (2π × C_gs_cas). For g_m = 1 mA/V and C_gs = 50 fF: pole at 3.2 GHz. For 10 cascode transistors in the chain, pole clustering near GBW creates cumulative phase lag. Mirror pole at 50 MHz creates another 45° loss. True PM = 58° − 45° = 13°: consistent with 45% overshoot.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Simple 2-pole model: PM = 58° → damping ratio ζ = PM/100 = 0.58 → overshoot = e^(−πζ/√(1−ζ²)) = e^(−1.99) = 13.7%. Measured: 45%. Discrepancy: hidden poles add ~45° of phase lag at gain crossover. True PM ≈ 58° − 45° = 13°. At PM = 13°: ζ ≈ 0.13, overshoot ≈ 70%. Measured 45% indicates some damping from resistive parasitics. Ringing frequency 45 MHz ≈ GBW/√3 for low-PM system. ✓

Fix: Reduce cascode and active load transistor sizes → push parasitic poles higher. Add R_z = 500 Ω in series with Cc → places a zero at f_z = 1/(2π × R_z × Cc) = 159 MHz that compensates the mirror pole phase loss. Also: add small capacitors C_add at internal nodes to create explicit poles at known frequencies (better than parasitic uncertainties).

Debug

Run a large-step transient simulation. If overshoot exceeds 2× what the PM formula predicts, hidden poles are present. To find them: simulate AC with a probe injected at different internal nodes (cascode outputs, mirror gate). Plot impedance vs frequency. Hidden poles appear as resonances in the impedance plot.

Problem 11 — The Differential Pair That Amplifies The Wrong Signal (ECG)

Problem Statement

ECG amplifier. Diff pair with active load. Electrode 1 → V_in+, Electrode 2 → V_in−. Signal: 1 mV ECG differential, 100 mV 50 Hz common mode. Measured: 50 Hz signal 100× larger than ECG. CMRR = 80 dB. The amplifier should reject 50 Hz by 10,000:1. But it doesn't. Why?

Intuition

The amplifier's CMRR is perfectly fine. But the electrode skin-impedance mismatch converts the 100 mV common-mode signal into a differential signal before it even reaches the amplifier. This converted differential signal is then amplified with full gain. CMRR is irrelevant once common-mode has already become differential. You cannot fight converted noise — you must prevent the conversion.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Electrode impedance mismatch: \( \Delta Z_e = 10\% \times Z_e = 10\% \times 3.2\,\text{kΩ} = 320\,\Omega \) at 50 Hz. Differential voltage from mismatch: \[ V_{diff} = V_{cm} \times \frac{\Delta Z_e}{Z_e} \approx 100\,\text{mV} \times 0.1 = 10\,\text{mV} \] Amplified by A_d = 1000: V_out = 10 V. ECG signal (1 mV × 1000 = 1 V) is overwhelmed.

Fix — Driven Right Leg (DRL) circuit: Sense common mode at the body, invert, amplify ×100, and feed back to the patient's right leg. This actively cancels the common-mode voltage at the body, reducing it from 100 mV to less than 1 mV. Then: mismatch-converted differential = 0.001 × 0.1 = 0.1 µV. Negligible. DRL is mandatory in every ECG machine for this reason.

Debug

Measure with both electrodes on the same skin location (zero differential signal, same common mode, zero impedance mismatch). If 50 Hz appears: CMRR is the problem. Connect both electrodes to a voltage divider (known mismatch ΔZ). If 50 Hz scales with ΔZ: electrode mismatch is the problem.

Problem 12 — Why The Op-Amp's Output Impedance Matters For CMRR

Problem Statement

Instrumentation amplifier (three op-amp topology). Stage 1: two non-inverting buffers. Stage 2: differential amplifier. Stage 1 op-amps: CMRR = 100 dB each. Stage 2 op-amp: CMRR = 80 dB. Overall INA CMRR = 60 dB. Expected: above 100 dB. Why is overall CMRR only 60 dB?

Intuition

The overall INA CMRR is limited by the weakest link. In the three-op-amp INA, the Stage 2 differential amplifier's resistor matching dominates. Even 0.1% resistors give only 60 dB CMRR from resistor mismatch — regardless of how good the op-amp's internal CMRR is.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Stage 2 differential amplifier with resistor mismatch ΔR/R = 0.1%: \[ A_{cm,stage2} \approx \frac{\Delta R}{R} = 0.001 \] CMRR from resistors alone: \[ \text{CMRR}_{R} = \frac{A_d}{A_{cm}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000 = 60\,\text{dB} \] This sets the floor regardless of op-amp CMRR. For 80 dB CMRR: need 0.01% resistors. For 100 dB: laser-trimmed thin-film resistors inside the IC. This is why precision INA ICs (INA128, AD8221) use internal resistors — external discrete implementations always lose 20–40 dB of CMRR to resistor mismatch.

Practical Implementation

0.1% resistors → 60 dB. 0.01% → 80 dB. Laser-trimmed IC → 100+ dB. Use a monolithic INA IC for any application requiring CMRR above 80 dB. For prototyping: use matched resistor networks from the same IC package (Vishay ACAM series: 0.01% tracking).

Problem 13 — Fully Differential Op-Amp: Why Both Outputs Must Be Used

Problem Statement

Fully differential op-amp (FD). Engineer connects V_in+ to signal, grounds V_in−, takes output from V_out+ only. Result: gain is correct but output has large DC offset AND poor SNR. What went wrong?

Intuition

A fully differential op-amp is designed to drive a differential ADC. It outputs two signals: V_out+ and V_out−. Each carries half the signal centered around V_OCM. Taking only V_out+ gives half the signal voltage but the same noise — 6 dB SNR loss. Also, the DC level of V_out+ is V_OCM (usually VDD/2) — this is not an error, but engineers expecting a zero-volt DC output are surprised.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

FD op-amp output: \[ V_{out+} = V_{OCM} + \frac{A_d (V_{in+} - V_{in-})}{2} \] Single-ended signal at V_out+: half of A_d × V_id. Differential signal: full A_d × V_id. SNR loss: 6 dB (factor of 2 in signal, same noise). DC offset: V_OCM (= VDD/2 = 1.65 V for 3.3 V supply). Fix: always use V_out+ − V_out− differentially. Connect V_OCM to ADC common-mode input. For single-ended output: use a standard op-amp instead of FD.

Problem 14 — BJT vs MOSFET Differential Pair: Which Is Better And When?

Problem Statement

Precision sensor amplifier. Options: A) BJT diff pair, B) JFET diff pair, C) MOSFET diff pair. Spec: V_OS < 50 µV, I_B < 1 pA, noise < 5 nV/√Hz, CMRR > 100 dB. Which topology passes? Which fails? Which is the correct choice?

Intuition

No single transistor type wins all four specs simultaneously. BJT wins on noise (g_m/I is highest) and loses on bias current (I_B = I_C/β = 100 nA). MOSFET wins on bias current (femtoamp gate leakage) and loses on noise (higher V_n) and offset (worse V_th matching). JFET is in the middle: better noise than MOSFET, much better bias current than BJT. The spec that is hardest to meet (I_B < 1 pA) eliminates BJT, making MOSFET or JFET the only candidates.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

BJT noise at I_C = 1 mA: \[ V_n = \sqrt{4kT/g_m} = \sqrt{4 \times 4.14\times10^{-21} / 38.5\times10^{-3}} = 0.66\,\text{nV/}\sqrt{\text{Hz}} \] Passes noise. I_B = 100 nA. Fails I_B spec by 100,000×. MOSFET noise at G_m = 1 mA/V: \[ V_n = \sqrt{8kT\gamma/(3G_m)} = \sqrt{8 \times 4.14\times10^{-21} \times 0.67 / (3\times10^{-3})} = 3.32\,\text{nV/}\sqrt{\text{Hz}} \] Passes noise. I_G < 1 fA. Passes I_B. Winner: MOSFET for this specific spec set. Use OPA2140 (JFET input, V_OS = 25 µV, I_B = 10 pA, V_n = 5 nV/√Hz). All specs met.

Problem 15 — The Multi-Stage Amplifier That Oscillates Only At Night

Problem Statement

Precision 3-stage amplifier. In lab: stable. At customer: works during day, oscillates at night. Oscillation at 3 MHz. Stops when operator touches the case. Occurs only below 18°C.

Intuition

BJT beta (β) increases as temperature decreases. More β = more gain per stage. Three stages compound: total gain = A1 × A2 × A3. If A1 rises 30% at cold temperature, total gain rises 30%³ − 1 ≈ 2.2× in open-loop gain. The gain crossover moves to a higher frequency where the phase is more negative, reducing phase margin below zero. Touching the case adds 100 pF body capacitance, loading the high-impedance node and reducing effective gain — killing the oscillation.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

At 25°C: total gain = 30³ = 27,000 = 89 dB. PM = 55°. At 10°C: β increases 30% → gain per stage → 39. Total = 39³ = 59,319 = 95.5 dB. Extra 6.5 dB of gain. Gain crossover moves to higher frequency. Extra phase lag at new crossover: assume 60°. New PM = 55° − 60° = −5°. Oscillation. Fix: emitter degeneration resistors. A_v = R_C/R_E — independent of β. β variation no longer affects gain → PM stays constant across temperature.

Practical Implementation

Standard qualification: test at −40°C, 0°C, 25°C, 85°C minimum (military: −55°C to +125°C). Emitter degeneration R_E: set to R_C/A_v_desired. For A_v = 10: R_E = R_C/10 = 1 kΩ for R_C = 10 kΩ. Phase margin at all temperatures: re-simulate with β at ±3σ process corners and at −40°C and +85°C.

Problem 16 — Noise Figure of a Receive Chain (Friis Formula)

Problem Statement

Receiver chain: LNA (G=20 dB, NF=1.5 dB) → Filter (loss=2 dB) → Mixer (G=−6 dB, NF=8 dB) → IF Amp (G=30 dB, NF=4 dB). What is the total system noise figure? Can it be improved by a better IF amplifier?

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Problem 21 — The Op-Amp That Works Perfectly Until You Add Gain

Problem Statement

Op-amp in unity gain: perfect, PM = 75°. Gain = 10 (R_f = 9 kΩ, R_in = 1 kΩ): slight ringing, PM ~45°. Gain = 100 (R_f = 99 kΩ, R_in = 1 kΩ): strong oscillation, PM < 0°. Higher gain should mean less bandwidth and more stability. Why does more gain cause less stability?

Intuition

The key insight is noise gain vs signal gain. Stability depends on where the A_OL curve intersects the noise gain line, not the signal gain. A decompensated op-amp has a "bump" (resonant peak) in its open-loop gain at intermediate frequencies. At high noise gain, the intersection falls right in the bump region where phase is most negative — conditional instability. At unity gain and at very high noise gain, the intersection is outside the bump and the system is stable.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

For inverting amplifier with signal gain −G: Noise gain = 1 + G. Signal gain = 10 → noise gain = 11. Signal gain = 100 → noise gain = 101. At gain crossover (A_OL = noise gain): f_cross = GBW / noise_gain. At noise_gain = 1 (unity): f_cross = GBW (high frequency, smooth rolloff, PM = 75°). At noise_gain = 101: f_cross = GBW/101 (lower frequency, lands in the bump, PM < 0°). The bump in A_OL is caused by a pole-zero pair (non-dominant pole + zero) near GBW/10. Fix: add feedback capacitor C_f across R_f. At high frequency: C_f makes noise gain = 1, shifting crossover to high frequency where A_OL is well-behaved. PM is restored.

Practical Implementation

Decompensated op-amps (LM318, AD8099): specify minimum stable gain in datasheet. LM318: stable for noise gain ≥ 5. Using LM318 at noise gain 3: oscillation. At noise gain 10: stable. C_f = 1/(2π × R_f × f_GBW/noise_gain) restores PM for any noise gain.

Problem 22 — Current Mirror That Temperature Compensates Itself

Problem Statement

BJT current mirror: Q1 (reference), Q2 (output). At 25°C: I_out = 1.02 mA (+2%). At 75°C: I_out = 0.99 mA (−1%). At 0°C: I_out = 1.05 mA (+5%). The error decreases at high temperature. Engineer claims self-compensation. What is really happening?

Intuition

BJT beta (β) increases with temperature. In a simple BJT mirror, the error is caused by finite beta — base currents are stolen from the reference, causing I_out to be slightly different from I_ref. As temperature rises and β increases, less base current is stolen and the error decreases. This looks like self-compensation but is actually β-related error reduction. It is not a designed compensation — it is coincidental and will vary part-to-part.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Simple BJT mirror error from finite β: \[ \frac{I_{out} - I_{ref}}{I_{ref}} \approx \frac{2}{\beta} \] At 25°C with β = 100: error = +2%. At 75°C with β = 200 (increased): error = +1%. At 0°C with β = 50 (decreased): error = +4%. Trend matches measured. True temperature compensation requires: bandgap reference (cancels V_BE CTAT with PTAT) for V_ref stability, plus PTAT current source for I_ref = V_ref/R stability. Wilson mirror reduces β sensitivity: \( \text{error}_{Wilson} \approx 2/\beta^2 \). For β = 100: error = 0.02%. Insensitive to β variation.

Problem 23 — The LDO That Oscillates With Ceramic Capacitors

Problem Statement

LDO: 5 V → 3.3 V, rated for 10 to 100 µF output capacitor. With 22 µF electrolytic (ESR = 0.3 Ω): stable. With 22 µF ceramic (ESR = 0.005 Ω): oscillates at 200 kHz. Same capacitance, different ESR. The ceramic cap is supposedly better. Why does it cause oscillation?

Intuition

An LDO's feedback loop has a dominant pole and a high-frequency pole from the pass transistor. Without an ESR zero, the phase hits −180° before the gain reaches 0 dB — instability. The ESR zero from the capacitor's equivalent series resistance provides critical phase boost at the gain crossover. A low-ESR ceramic cap pushes this zero to megahertz range — too late to help at the LDO's 50 kHz crossover. The electrolytic's "inferior" 0.3 Ω ESR is actually essential for stability.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Stable. Ceramic: f_z = 1/(2π × 0.005 × 22µ) = 1.45 MHz. Far above crossover: φ_boost = arctan(50k/1.45M) = 2°. PM ≈ 5°. Unstable. Fix: add R_series = 0.3 Ω in series with ceramic cap to recreate the ESR zero. Or use a modern LDO with internal compensation (TPS7A20, LT3080) that is stable with any capacitor.

Problem 26 — Class AB Bias That Causes Thermal Runaway

Problem Statement

Class AB audio amplifier. I_Q = 20 mA at 25°C. At 50% power: transistors at 60°C, I_Q rises to 35 mA. At 100% power: transistors at 125°C, I_Q = 80 mA and rising rapidly. Thermal runaway occurs unless music stops.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Thermal runaway. With perfect coupling: V_bias − V_BE_out = constant → I_Q = constant. Emitter degeneration R_E = 0.5 Ω: as I_Q rises, V_RE = I_Q × R_E rises → V_BE_effective drops → I_Q self-stabilizes. Stable condition: ∂I_Q/∂T = 0 requires R_E × (dI_Q/dT) = −dV_BE_out/dT = +2 mV/°C. Scenario 2 — Microphone Preamp Makes Singer Sound Distorted at High Volume

Problem Statement

Electret microphone → preamp → mixer. At low volume: THD < 0.05%. At high volume: THD = 3%. Not clipping at output (checked on oscilloscope). Distortion is harsh, odd harmonics dominant.

Intuition

The output is not clipping but an internal node is. In a two-stage op-amp with ±5 V supply, the internal node between stages may only swing ±2 V. With high input level and high first-stage gain, the internal node clips. The second stage amplifies the clipped waveform, producing harsh odd-order distortion (square wave has odd harmonics). The fix is to reduce gain-per-stage, switch to a higher-supply op-amp, or use a rail-to-rail design.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

At gain = 100, ±5 V supply, A_v1 ≈ 10 internally: V_internal = 10 × V_in. For V_in = 200 mV: V_internal = 2 V — right at the edge. At V_in = 250 mV: V_internal = 2.5 V → clips at ±2 V → output = clipped amplified. THD rises dramatically. Fix: reduce gain per stage to 10 each (10 × 10 = 100 total). Each stage: ±5 V internal headroom for ±500 mV input. At 250 mV input: V_internal1 = 2.5 V < 5 V. No clipping. Or: use op-amp rated for ±5 V (OPA2134, GBW = 8 MHz): internal swing up to ±4.5 V. Problem solved.

Scenario 3 — Battery-Powered Device Runs 2 Hours Instead of 24 Hours

Problem Statement

Wearable health monitor. Battery 100 mAh. Target 24 hours (design calculated 4.2 mA average). Actual: 2 hours (measured 50 mA). 12× more current than designed.

Intuition

Excess current means parallel current paths that were not accounted for. The diagnostic approach: insert a series ammeter in each major supply branch to find which sub-circuit consumes the extra current. The culprits in this scenario: a bias resistor that was placed at the wrong value (100 Ω instead of 100 kΩ = 1000× wrong), unnecessary pull-down resistors at op-amp outputs, and high-quiescent-current op-amps (LM741) that could be replaced with micropower parts.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Before fix: I_MCU = 0.2 mA, I_sensor = 0.1 mA, I_wrong_R = 3.3 V / 100 Ω = 33 mA, I_OpAmp_IQ = 3 × 1.7 mA = 5.1 mA, I_load = 3 × 3.3 mA = 9.9 mA. Total = 48.3 mA. After fix: I_wrong_R → 33 µA (100 kΩ), I_OpAmp → 0.06 µA (OPA2333 + sleep mode), I_load → removed. Total = 0.333 mA. Battery life: 100 mAh / 0.333 mA = 300 hours. 12× above 24-hour target.

Scenario 4 — Audio Amplifier Hums When Touching Volume Knob

Problem Statement

Stereo amplifier. Clean audio during playback. User touches metal volume knob: 60 Hz hum appears. Increases with firmer contact. Worse near fluorescent lights.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Human body couples to 60 Hz via body capacitance C_body ≈ 100 pF. Current injected: I = V_60Hz × 2π × 60 Hz × C_body = 120 V × 377 × 100 pF = 4.5 µA (peak). At high-Z amplifier input (100 kΩ): V_noise = 4.5 µA × 100 kΩ = 450 mV. Audible and large. Fix 1 (preferred): use plastic shaft potentiometer — breaks the capacitive coupling path. Fix 2: ground the metal knob shaft to signal ground. Body touching shaft → current flows safely to GND, not through signal path. Fix 3: add R_guard = 1 kΩ from wiper to GND — reduces V_noise to 4.5 µA × 1 kΩ = 4.5 mV. Barely audible at low volume.

Scenario 5 — pH Sensor Reads Correctly at 25°C But Fails at 80°C

Problem Statement

pH sensor for industrial process control. Readings correct at 25°C. At 80°C: readings drift by +1.5 pH units. After cooling to 25°C: readings return to correct values. Sensor is rated to 120°C.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Nernst equation: \[ V_{pH} = E_{ref} + \frac{RT}{nF} \times 2.303 \times (-\text{pH}) \] Sensitivity at 25°C (298 K): \( S_{25} = 8.314 \times 298 / 96485 \times 2.303 = 59.16\,\text{mV/pH} \). At 80°C (353 K): \( S_{80} = 59.16 \times 353/298 = 70.12\,\text{mV/pH} \). Gain calibrated at 25°C: reads pH × (S_80/S_25) = pH × 1.185. Error = 0.185 × 8 = 1.48 pH ≈ 1.5 pH. ✓ Fix: add temperature sensor + PTC in gain-setting resistor R_G. As T rises: R_G increases → gain decreases ∝ 1/T → compensates Nernst factor. Or: software compensation using the measured temperature.

Scenario 6 — Oscilloscope Shows No Gate Signal But MOSFET LED Blinks

Problem Statement

MCU generates 1 MHz PWM to drive a high-side MOSFET. LED blinks at 1 Hz. Oscilloscope on MOSFET gate: flat line at 2 V. Expected: 0–12 V switching at 1 MHz.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Gate RC time constant: τ = R_g × C_gate = 10 kΩ × 5 nF = 50 µs. At 1 MHz (period = 1 µs): voltage change per cycle = 12 V × (1 − e^(−0.5µs/50µs)) = 12 × 0.01 = 0.12 V. Gate barely moves (oscilloscope sees ~2 V average). MOSFET partially ON (partially conducting) during the brief charging → LED is dimly but continuously lit, blinking at the MCU's 1 Hz duty cycle. Fix: R_g < t_half / (5 × C_gate) = 0.5 µs / (5 × 5 nF) = 20 Ω. Use R_g = 10 Ω + gate driver IC (TC4420, R_out = 1 Ω). τ = 1 Ω × 5 nF = 5 ns → gate fully charges in 25 ns < 500 ns half-period. ✓

Scenario 7 — 12-bit ADC Gives Only 8-bit Resolution in Final Product

Problem Statement

12-bit ADC, ENOB = 11.8 bits in lab. Same PCB with all circuits: ENOB = 7.9 bits. Switching regulator (200 kHz), MCU (72 MHz), motor driver (20 kHz) are active.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

ENOB loss: 11.8 − 7.9 = 3.9 bits. Each bit = 6 dB, so noise floor raised by 4 × 6 = 24 dB = 16×. The 200 kHz switcher is the main culprit (2.3-bit loss identified in step testing). Fix layers: (1) Series ferrite + parallel 10µF/100nF/10nF on ADC VCC. (2) R = 10 Ω + 10µF/100nF/10nF on ADC VREF. (3) 4th-order Butterworth LPF at f_c = ADC_rate/5 = 20 kHz — attenuates 200 kHz by (200k/20k)^4 × correction ≈ 64 dB → recovers ~10 bits. (4) Ground plane split: digital/analog separated at star point under ADC. (5) Sync ADC conversions between PWM switching edges. Expected ENOB after fix: >11 bits.

Scenario 8 — Motor Controller Causes Bluetooth to Drop Connection

Problem Statement

PCB with BLDC motor controller (FOC, 20 kHz switching) + ESP32 (Bluetooth/WiFi). Bluetooth works when motor is off. Drops every 2–3 minutes at low speed; every 30 seconds at full torque.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Motor EMI analysis: phase switch dV/dt = VDC / t_rise. With VDC = 48 V and t_rise = 20 ns: dV/dt = 2.4 GV/s. Radiated EMI at harmonics of 20 kHz extends into 2.4 GHz (Bluetooth band) via 120th harmonic of 20 kHz = 2.4 GHz! Fix layer 1 (easy): separate 5 V regulator (LDO) for ESP32 with 40 dB PSRR at 20 kHz. Fix layer 2 (medium): increase R_gate from 10 to 47 Ω → dV/dt reduced 4.7×, spectral content at 2.4 GHz drops ~20 dB. Fix layer 3 (medium): common-mode choke on all 3 motor phase wires → blocks CM switching currents (EMI source). Fix layer 4 (hard): move ESP32 antenna to corner farthest from motor driver, add ground plane shield layer.

Scenario 9 — Precision Scale Drifts Upward 5 Grams Over 10 Minutes

Problem Statement

Load cell scale, 0.1 g resolution, 5 kg capacity. Powers on correctly. After 10 minutes: reads 5.0 g with nothing on the scale. Electronics temperature rises 15°C over 10 minutes.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Load cell sensitivity: 2 mV/V × 5 V = 10 mV full scale for 5 kg. 5 g = 0.1% of 5 kg → output shift = 0.1% × 10 mV × 1000(gain) = 10 mV. Excitation drift: LDO tempco ≈ 50 ppm/°C. Over 15°C: ΔV_exc = 5 V × 50 ppm × 15 = 3.75 mV. Output shift = 3.75 mV × (V_bridge/V_exc) × gain = 3.75 mV × 0.002 × 1000 = 7.5 mV. Close to measured 10 mV shift. Fix — ratiometric measurement: connect ADC VREF to V_excitation/2 (via op-amp follower). Then: any drift in V_exc is divided out in the ADC ratio. V_exc drifts 1% → bridge output drifts 1% → VREF drifts 1% → ADC reading unchanged. Complete drift cancellation.

Scenario 10 — RF Power Amplifier Fails FCC Harmonics Test

Problem Statement

2.4 GHz WiFi PA. Output: +20 dBm at 2.4 GHz. 2nd harmonic (4.8 GHz): −22 dBm (must be < −41.2 dBm). 3rd harmonic (7.2 GHz): −38 dBm. Both fail FCC Part 15. Harmonics from PA are at −35 dBc.

Mathematics — Complete Solution

Need < −41.2 dBm at 4.8 GHz. Current: −22 dBm. Need 19.2 dB more attenuation. 5th-order Butterworth LPF at f_c = 2.5 GHz. Attenuation at 4.8 GHz (2× passband): for 5th order: \[ \text{att} = N \times 20\,\text{dB/decade} \times \log_2(2) = 5 \times 6\,\text{dB} = 30\,\text{dB} > 19.2\,\text{dB} \] ✓ At 7.2 GHz (3×): 5 × 20 × log(3) = 47.7 dB > 19.2 dB. ✓ Component values (50 Ω system): L1 = L5 = 0.618 × 50/(2π × 2.5G) = 1.97 nH, C2 = C4 = 1.618/(50 × 2π × 2.5G) = 2.06 pF, L3 = 2.000 × 50/(2π × 2.5G) = 6.37 nH. The Five Master Insights of Phase 4

  1. Gain requires r_out. r_out requires cascode. Cascode requires headroom. This triangle defines the fundamental topology choice for every application.
  2. Stability requires knowledge of ALL poles — not just the compensated two-stage poles. Every capacitance in the circuit contributes. Bode plot alone is not enough; transient verification is mandatory.
  3. Bandwidth = G_m / (2π C). Power buys G_m. Topology minimizes C. Layout minimizes C. Three tools to improve bandwidth: more power, better topology, careful layout.
  4. Noise = 8kT / (3G_m). The input stage is everything. All other stages contribute noise divided by the gain preceding them. Friis formula applies to analog amplifiers as much as to RF receivers.
  5. Matching = area. Better matching requires larger transistors. Larger transistors mean more capacitance, lower bandwidth. This is the fundamental matching–bandwidth trade-off in all analog IC design.
